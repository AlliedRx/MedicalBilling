Ethics vs. Morals

Understanding the foundation of ethical decision-making in healthcare.

Key Definitions

Concept Definition Source Healthcare Application
Morals Personal beliefs about right and wrong Personal values, religion, upbringing Your personal views on abortion, euthanasia, etc.
Ethics Systematic study of moral principles Professional codes, societal norms Following HIPAA, maintaining confidentiality
Professional Ethics Standards of conduct for a profession Professional organizations, laws AAMA Code of Ethics, nursing codes

Personal vs. Professional Ethics

Personal Ethics

  • Individual beliefs and values
  • Based on personal experiences
  • May vary between individuals
  • Often tied to culture/religion

Example: Personally believing life begins at conception

Professional Ethics

  • Standardized code of conduct
  • Based on professional standards
  • Consistent across practitioners
  • Legally and professionally enforced

Example: Providing non-judgmental care regardless of personal views

Separation Plan: Personal vs. Professional

Developing strategies to maintain professional objectivity:

Self-Awareness

Recognize your personal biases and values

Professional Boundaries

Maintain appropriate patient-provider relationships

Consultation

Seek guidance from supervisors or ethics committees

Referral Options

When conflicts cannot be resolved, arrange alternative care

Four Ethical Principles

The foundation of ethical decision-making in healthcare.

The Four Principles

1. Autonomy

Definition: Respect for patient self-determination and decision-making

Key Actions: Informed consent, truth-telling, confidentiality

Example: Respecting a patient's refusal of treatment even if you disagree

Related: Advance Directives, Living Wills

2. Beneficence

Definition: Acting in the patient's best interest

Key Actions: Promoting good, preventing harm, removing harm

Example: Recommending a treatment that benefits the patient

Related: Patient Advocacy, Quality Care

3. Nonmaleficence

Definition: "First, do no harm" (primum non nocere)

Key Actions: Avoiding harm, minimizing risks

Example: Double-checking medication dosages to prevent errors

Related: Malpractice Prevention, Safety Protocols

4. Justice

Definition: Fairness in distribution of resources and treatment

Key Actions: Equality, fairness, non-discrimination

Example: Treating all patients equally regardless of ability to pay

Related: Resource Allocation, Access to Care

Principle Conflicts & Balancing

Sometimes ethical principles conflict. Healthcare professionals must balance them:

Case Study: Conflict Resolution

Situation: A patient with a highly contagious disease refuses isolation (autonomy), but this puts others at risk (nonmaleficence).

Balancing Approach:
  1. Respect autonomy through education and persuasion
  2. If refusal continues, consider public health laws (justice)
  3. Document all attempts at resolution
  4. Involve public health authorities if necessary

Specific Ethical Issues

Applying ethical principles to complex healthcare situations.

Genetics Ethics

Key Issues in Genetics:

  • Genetic Testing: Privacy, discrimination (GINA protections)
  • Informed Consent: Understanding implications of genetic information
  • Family Implications: Testing affects biological relatives
  • Prenatal Testing: Decisions about pregnancy continuation
Appropriate Responses:
  1. Provide complete, unbiased information about genetic testing
  2. Respect patient autonomy in testing decisions
  3. Maintain strict confidentiality of genetic information
  4. Refer to genetic counselors when appropriate
  5. Educate about GINA protections against discrimination

Reproductive Ethics

Key Issues

  • Contraception access
  • Abortion services
  • Assisted reproductive technologies
  • Sterilization decisions
  • Prenatal testing choices

Professional Response

  • Non-judgmental care regardless of personal views
  • Provide accurate medical information
  • Respect patient autonomy in decisions
  • Know facility policies and referral options
  • Maintain professional boundaries

End of Life Ethics

Ethical considerations in terminal care and death.

Kübler-Ross Stages of Grief

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross identified five stages of grief that dying patients and their families may experience:

1

Denial

"This can't be happening to me."

2

Anger

"Why me? This isn't fair!"

3

Bargaining

"If I do X, maybe I'll get better."

4

Depression

Withdrawal, sadness, grief

5

Acceptance

Coming to terms with reality

Note: Not everyone experiences all stages, and they don't necessarily occur in order. The model helps understand common emotional responses.

Organ Donation Ethics

Patient Self-Determination Act (PSDA)

Requires healthcare facilities to inform patients about advance directives and document their wishes.

Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (UAGA)

Governs organ donation procedures and establishes registry systems.

Key Ethical Principles

Autonomy (respecting donor wishes), beneficence (saving lives), justice (fair allocation).

Advance Directives & Related Terms

Advance Directive: Legal document outlining healthcare wishes if patient becomes incapacitated.
Living Will: Specific instructions about end-of-life care (ventilators, feeding tubes, etc.).
Medical Durable Power of Attorney: Designates someone to make medical decisions if patient cannot.
Healthcare Proxy: Person authorized to make healthcare decisions (same as medical power of attorney).
DNR/DNI: Do Not Resuscitate/Do Not Intubate orders.
POLST/MOLST: Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (medical order, not just preference).

End-of-Life Scenario

Situation: A terminally ill patient has a DNR order but family members are demanding "everything be done."

Appropriate Response:
  1. Review the advance directive/DNR order
  2. Facilitate family conference with healthcare team
  3. Respect patient's documented wishes (autonomy)
  4. Provide emotional support to family (beneficence)
  5. Document all discussions and decisions

Building blocks of healthcare communication.

Latin Origins

~38% of medical terms

Pulmonary (pulmo = lung)

Other Languages

~14% of medical terms

Alcohol (Arabic)
Quinine (Quechua)

Decodable vs. Nondecodable Terms

Type Definition Examples Learning Approach
Decodable Terms Can be broken into word parts with specific meanings Cardi/o/logy, Hepat/itis, Sub/cutane/ous Learn word parts and rules
Nondecodable Terms Cannot be broken into meaningful word parts Asthma, Diabetes, Measles Memorization required

CARD Method for Decoding Terms

C - Check

Check if term is decodable (has recognizable word parts)

A - Assign

Assign meanings to each word part (prefix, root, suffix)

R - Reverse

Read the meaning backward (suffix first, then prefix, then root)

D - Define

Define the term based on combined meanings

CARD Example: "Subcutaneous"

  1. C: Decodable (sub- + cutane + -ous)
  2. A: sub- = under, cutane = skin, -ous = pertaining to
  3. R: pertaining to under the skin
  4. D: Definition: Under the skin

Building & Spelling Rules

Combining Vowel Rules
  • Use "o" when combining two roots
  • Omit combining vowel before a suffix starting with a vowel
  • Keep combining vowel between two roots
Pluralization Rules
  • -a becomes -ae (vertebra → vertebrae)
  • -is becomes -es (diagnosis → diagnoses)
  • -um becomes -a (bacterium → bacteria)
  • -us becomes -i (nucleus → nuclei)

Anatomy Basics

Structural organization and directional terminology.

Structural Organization

From simplest to most complex:

Chemical Level

Atoms and molecules (DNA, proteins, carbohydrates)

Cellular Level

Basic unit of life (muscle cells, nerve cells, blood cells)

Tissue Level

Groups of similar cells (epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous)

Organ Level

Two or more tissue types (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys)

Organ System Level

Related organs working together (cardiovascular, respiratory)

Organism Level

The entire living person

Directional Terminology

Superior/Inferior

Above/Below

Anterior/Posterior

Front/Back

Medial/Lateral

Toward midline/Away from midline

The nose is medial to the eyes

Proximal/Distal

Closer to trunk/Farther from trunk

The elbow is proximal to the wrist

Superficial/Deep

Toward surface/Away from surface

Cephalad/Caudad

Toward head/Toward tail

Used in embryology and radiology

Body Planes & Cavities

Body Planes

  • Sagittal: Divides left and right
  • Coronal/Frontal: Divides anterior and posterior
  • Transverse/Axial: Divides superior and inferior
  • Oblique: Diagonal cuts

Major Body Cavities

  • Cranial: Contains brain
  • Spinal: Contains spinal cord
  • Thoracic: Contains heart, lungs
  • Abdominopelvic: Contains digestive and reproductive organs

Body Organization & Systems

Major organ systems and their functions.

Major Organ Systems

Cardiovascular System

Heart, blood vessels - transports nutrients and oxygen

Respiratory System

Nervous System

Endocrine System

Acid-Base Balance

pH Scale

The body maintains pH between 7.35-7.45

7.35-7.45

Acidic: pH < 7.35

Alkaline: pH > 7.45

Regulation Systems

  • Chemical Buffers: Immediate response
  • Respiratory System: Controls CO2 (minutes)
  • Renal System: Controls bicarbonate (hours-days)

Acid-Base Disorders

Acidosis: pH < 7.35 - Can cause confusion, fatigue, shortness of breath

Alkalosis: pH > 7.45 - Can cause muscle twitching, nausea, confusion

Pathology Basics

Understanding disease processes and terminology.

Pathology Terminology

Pathology: Study of disease
Etiology: Cause of disease
Pathogenesis: Development of disease
Signs: Objective findings (fever, rash)
Symptoms: Subjective experiences (pain, nausea)
Syndrome: Collection of signs and symptoms
Diagnosis: Identification of disease
Prognosis: Predicted outcome of disease
Acute: Sudden onset, short duration
Chronic: Long duration, slow progression

Protection Mechanisms

Physical Barriers

Skin, mucous membranes, cilia

Chemical Barriers

Stomach acid, enzymes, antibodies

Immune Response

White blood cells, inflammation, fever

Behavioral Defenses

Avoidance of hazards, hygiene practices

Predisposing Factors & Disease Causes

Predisposing Factors

  • Age: Very young and elderly more susceptible
  • Gender: Some diseases affect genders differently
  • Genetics: Family history increases risk
  • Lifestyle: Diet, exercise, smoking, alcohol
  • Environment: Pollution, workplace hazards

Disease Causes

  • Infectious: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
  • Genetic: Inherited disorders (cystic fibrosis)
  • Congenital: Present at birth (birth defects)
  • Degenerative: Wear and tear (osteoarthritis)
  • Neoplastic: Tumors (benign or malignant)
