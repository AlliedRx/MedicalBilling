1 Fundamental Writing Skills

Capitalization Guidelines

  • Always capitalize proper nouns (names, specific places, organizations)
  • Capitalize titles when they precede a name: Dr. Smith, Professor Johnson
  • Capitalize medical terms when they are proper nouns: Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease
  • Capitalize department names when they are specific: Cardiology Department
  • Do not capitalize common nouns: the doctor, the hospital

Number Usage Guidelines

  • Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above
  • Always use numerals for ages, measurements, dosages, and lab values
  • Use numerals for dates, times, and monetary amounts
  • For percentages, use numerals followed by % symbol: 15%

Punctuation Guidelines

  • Use commas to separate items in a series
  • Use semicolons to connect related independent clauses
  • Use periods to end declarative sentences
  • Use colons to introduce lists or explanations
  • Avoid excessive exclamation points in professional communication

4 Templates in Professional Communication

Templates serve several important purposes in healthcare communication:

Consistency

Ensures all documents follow the same format and contain required information

Efficiency

Saves time by providing a ready-made structure for common documents

Accuracy

Reduces errors by including all necessary components automatically

Compliance

Ensures documents meet legal and regulatory requirements (HIPAA, etc.)

Common Healthcare Templates: Referral letters, consultation requests, test result notifications, appointment reminders, billing inquiries, and patient instructions.

5 Memorandums & Email Etiquette

Memorandum Components

  • TO: Recipient(s)
  • FROM: Sender
  • DATE: Date memo is sent
  • SUBJECT: Brief description of memo content
  • Body: Clear, concise information
  • Signature: Initials or signature

Professional Email Etiquette

  • Use a clear, descriptive subject line
  • Address recipient professionally (Dear Dr. Smith)
  • Keep messages concise and to the point
  • Use proper grammar and spelling
  • Avoid ALL CAPS (perceived as shouting)
  • Include a professional signature block
  • Never discuss sensitive patient information in regular email HIPAA
  • Use encrypted email for protected health information
  • Proofread before sending
  • Respond within 24 hours during business days
Critical Reminder: Regular email is NOT secure for Protected Health Information (PHI). Always use encrypted email systems approved by your healthcare organization.

CONFIDENTIAL MEDICAL INFORMATION

FAX TRANSMISSION COVER SHEET

TO: [Recipient Name/Department]

FAX: [Recipient Fax Number]

FROM: [Your Name/Department]

DATE: [Date]

NUMBER OF PAGES: [Including this cover sheet]

CONFIDENTIALITY NOTICE: This transmission contains confidential information belonging to the sender. This information is legally protected under HIPAA regulations. If you are not the intended recipient, you are hereby notified that any disclosure, copying, distribution, or use of the contents is strictly prohibited. If you have received this transmission in error, please notify the sender immediately and destroy the original and any copies.

MESSAGE: [Brief description of contents]

IF TRANSMISSION PROBLEMS OCCUR, CALL: [Your Phone Number]

Fax Security Protocols:
  • Verify fax number before sending
  • Use pre-programmed numbers when possible
  • Confirm receipt with recipient
  • Place fax machine in secure location
  • Remove documents from fax tray immediately
  • Keep transmission logs for 6 years (HIPAA requirement)

7 Addressing Envelopes & Folding Documents

Envelope Addressing

  • Use block format with all caps
  • Include recipient's full name and professional title
  • Use correct postal abbreviations for states
  • Include return address in top left corner
  • Place postage in top right corner
  • For business: Include "ATTN:" for specific individuals

Standard Folding (for #10 envelope)

  1. Place letter face up
  2. Fold bottom third up toward top
  3. Fold top third down over first fold
  4. Insert with last fold (top edge) facing envelope flap

Window Envelope Folding

  1. Position letter so address shows through window
  2. Fold so address remains visible
  3. Ensure no other information shows through window

8 Postal Services in Ambulatory Care

First-Class Mail

Standard for letters, bills, statements. Delivery in 1-3 business days.

Priority Mail

Faster delivery (1-3 days) with tracking included. For time-sensitive documents.

Certified Mail

Provides proof of mailing and delivery. Essential for legal documents.

Return Receipt

Proof of delivery with recipient's signature. Often used with certified mail.

Registered Mail

Most secure option with chain of custody. For highly valuable documents.

Express Mail

Overnight delivery. For emergency medical records or time-critical items.

Mail Security: Always use opaque envelopes for medical information. Consider using "Confidential" stamps for sensitive documents.

9 Medical Assistant's Role with Incoming Mail

üì¨ Daily Mail Processing Procedure
  1. Sort mail by category: patient correspondence, insurance, invoices, lab results, pharmacy
  2. Open mail carefully (use letter opener, preserve contents)
  3. Date-stamp all incoming mail
  4. Sort by priority: Urgent (lab results, referrals), Routine (bills, general correspondence)
  5. Route to appropriate personnel: Clinical to providers, billing to financial department
  6. Document receipt in mail log if required
  7. Handle confidential items according to HIPAA protocols

Special Handling for Specific Mail Types

  • Lab Results: Route immediately to appropriate provider
  • Legal Documents: Do not open; deliver sealed to office manager
  • Prescription Refills: Attach to patient chart and flag for provider
  • Insurance Correspondence: Forward to billing department with date received noted
  • Patient Payments: Process immediately and provide receipt

Cell Phone Use in Healthcare Facilities

  • Personal cell phones should be on silent/vibrate in patient care areas
  • Never discuss PHI on personal cell phones
  • Use hospital-provided encrypted phones for work-related calls when mobile
  • Avoid using cell phones during direct patient interactions
  • Follow facility-specific policies on mobile device use

2 Effective Telephone Use

Essential Telephone Equipment

  • Multi-line telephone system with hold and transfer capabilities
  • Headsets for hands-free operation during documentation
  • Message pads or electronic message system
  • Speed dial directory for frequently called numbers
  • On-hold messaging system with healthcare information or music

Active Listening Skills

  • Give full attention to the caller
  • Avoid interrupting
  • Paraphrase to confirm understanding
  • Ask clarifying questions
  • Note important details
  • Provide appropriate verbal cues ("I see," "Go on")

Professional Telephone Techniques

Correct: "Good morning, City Medical Clinic, this is Sarah speaking. How may I help you?"

Incorrect: "Yeah, City Medical."

Tone of Voice & Enunciation

  • Speak clearly and at a moderate pace
  • Modulate tone to sound pleasant and professional
  • Smile while talking (it changes your tone)
  • Pronounce words completely
  • Avoid mumbling or speaking too quickly
  • Adjust volume appropriately (not too loud or soft)

3 Answering Procedures & Preparedness

üìã Thinking Ahead: Be Prepared Before Answering
  • Have message pads and pens ready
  • Keep frequently referenced information accessible (provider schedules, office hours)
  • Know emergency protocols and contact numbers
  • Have referral directories available
  • Keep computer system open to appointment scheduling or patient records

Correct Telephone Answering Procedure

  1. Answer within 3 rings
  2. Identify facility: "Good [morning/afternoon], [Clinic Name]"
  3. Identify yourself: "This is [Your Name]"
  4. Offer assistance: "How may I help you?"
  5. Listen attentively to caller's needs
  6. Respond appropriately based on call type
Professional Tip: Always answer with a smile in your voice. Callers can hear your facial expressions through your tone.

4 Screening Calls & Emergency Protocols

Screening Incoming Calls

  • Determine nature of call before transferring
  • Ask: "May I tell the doctor what this call is regarding?"
  • Triage calls: emergencies, urgent matters, routine questions
  • Screen out solicitors and sales calls appropriately
  • Respect caller privacy while gathering necessary information

Handling Emergency Calls

Emergency Call Protocol:
  1. Stay calm and speak clearly
  2. Ask: "What is the nature of your emergency?"
  3. Determine if immediate 911 call is needed
  4. If yes: Instruct caller to hang up and dial 911, or offer to connect them
  5. If no: Gather essential information quickly

Emergency Call Questions

  • What is the emergency? (Describe symptoms)
  • Is the person conscious and breathing?
  • What is the person's name and date of birth?
  • What is your location and phone number?
  • Are there any known allergies or medical conditions?
  • What medications is the person currently taking?
  • When did the symptoms begin?

5 Placing on Hold & Transferring Calls

Proper Hold Procedure

  1. Ask permission: "May I place you on hold for a moment?"
  2. Wait for response before placing on hold
  3. Return within 30-45 seconds
  4. If longer wait is needed: "Thank you for holding. I need a bit more time. Would you prefer to continue holding or may I call you back?"
  5. Thank caller for their patience when returning

Correct Call Transfer

  1. Explain why you're transferring: "Let me connect you with our billing department who can help with that."
  2. Provide recipient's name if known: "I'm transferring you to Ms. Johnson in billing."
  3. Stay on line to introduce caller if possible: "Ms. Johnson, I have Mr. Smith on the line regarding his insurance claim."
  4. If direct transfer isn't possible, provide extension number
  5. Ensure call goes through before disconnecting

Transfer Script: "I can help you with that. Let me transfer you to our referral coordinator who handles specialist appointments. Please hold while I connect you."

6 Taking Accurate Telephone Messages

The Seven Essential Elements

1. Date and Time
Include both date and exact time of call
2. Caller's Full Name
First and last name, spelled correctly
3. Caller's Phone Number
Include area code and best time to call back
4. Recipient's Name
Who the message is for
5. Message Content
Brief but complete summary of caller's message
6. Action Required
What needs to be done (call back, schedule, etc.)
7. Your Initials
Message taker's initials for accountability

Sample Message:

Date: 3/15/2024 | Time: 10:45 AM

For: Dr. Wilson

From: Robert Johnson (patient)

Phone: (555) 123-4567 (Best time: after 3 PM)

Message: Requesting refill of Lisinopril. Last filled 2/15/2024. Also reporting mild dizziness when standing quickly.

Action: Please call patient to discuss symptoms and refill.

Taken by: MJ

HIPAA Reminder: When leaving messages, never reveal protected health information. Simply state: "This is [Clinic] calling for [Patient Name]. Please call us back at [Phone Number]."
