1 Health Insurance Purpose & Cost-Sharing

Purpose of Health Insurance

  • Risk Pooling: Spreads financial risk across many people
  • Financial Protection: Prevents catastrophic medical expenses
  • Access to Care: Enables people to receive needed medical services
  • Preventive Care: Encourages regular check-ups and early intervention
  • Negotiated Rates: Insurance companies negotiate lower rates with providers

Cost-Sharing Concepts

Deductible

Amount patient pays out-of-pocket before insurance begins to pay

Example: $1,000 deductible means patient pays first $1,000 of covered services

Co-Payment (Co-Pay)

Fixed amount patient pays for specific services

Example: $20 for office visits, $50 for specialist visits

Co-Insurance

Percentage of costs patient pays after deductible is met

Example: 20% co-insurance means patient pays 20%, insurance pays 80%

Out-of-Pocket Maximum

Maximum amount patient pays in a year; after this, insurance pays 100%

Example: $5,000 out-of-pocket maximum protects against catastrophic costs

2 Government Health Insurance Plans

Medicare Federal

For: People 65+, certain disabilities, ESRD

Parts: A (Hospital), B (Medical), C (Advantage), D (Drugs)

Funding: Federal payroll taxes, premiums

Medicaid State/Federal

For: Low-income individuals and families

Coverage: Varies by state, comprehensive benefits

Funding: Federal and state governments

TRICARE Military

For: Active duty, retired military, families

Coverage: Comprehensive health benefits

Funding: Department of Defense

CHAMPVA Veterans

For: Veterans with disabilities, survivors

Coverage: Similar to TRICARE, cost-sharing

Funding: Department of Veterans Affairs

Medicare Parts Overview

  • Part A: Hospital insurance (inpatient, hospice, skilled nursing)
  • Part B: Medical insurance (outpatient, preventive, doctor services)
  • Part C: Medicare Advantage (private plans offering Parts A, B, often D)
  • Part D: Prescription drug coverage
  • Medigap: Supplemental policies to cover gaps in Parts A & B

3 Private Health Insurance Plans

Employer-Sponsored

  • Most common private insurance in US
  • Employer pays portion of premium
  • Various plan options (HMO, PPO, POS)
  • Coverage continues via COBRA if employment ends

Individual/Family Plans

  • Purchased directly from insurance company
  • Available through Health Insurance Marketplaces
  • May qualify for premium tax credits
  • Must meet ACA requirements for essential benefits

Association Health Plans

  • Groups of small businesses or associations
  • Purchasing power similar to large employers
  • May have fewer regulatory requirements
  • Coverage varies by association

4 Traditional (Fee-for-Service) Plans

Traditional Insurance Characteristics

  • Provider Choice: Patient can see any provider
  • Billing: Provider bills insurance for services rendered
  • Cost-Sharing: Deductible, co-insurance (typically 80/20)
  • No Network: No restricted provider networks
  • Claim Forms: Submit CMS-1500 or UB-04 forms
  • Pre-authorization: Often required for expensive services

How Fee-for-Service Works

  1. Patient receives medical services
  2. Provider bills insurance company
  3. Insurance evaluates claim against policy benefits
  4. Insurance pays percentage (typically 80%) of allowed amount
  5. Patient pays remaining percentage (typically 20%) plus any deductible
  6. Provider bills patient for any balance above allowed amount

5 Managed Care Models

Model Key Features Provider Choice Referral Required Cost to Patient
HMO (Health Maintenance Organization) Comprehensive care through network, PCP coordinates Must use network providers Yes (except emergencies) Lowest (co-pays only)
PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) Network with out-of-network options Any provider (higher cost for out-of-network) No Moderate (deductible + co-insurance)
POS (Point of Service) Hybrid of HMO and PPO features Any provider (PCP required for in-network benefits) Yes for in-network Varies by service type
EPO (Exclusive Provider Organization) Network only (like HMO) without PCP requirement Must use network providers No Low (deductible + co-insurance)

6 Managed Care Referrals & Utilization Management

Referral Process in Managed Care

1

Patient sees PCP

2

PCP determines need for specialist

3

PCP submits referral request to insurance

4

Insurance approves/denies referral

Documentation Requirements for Referrals

Referral Documentation Includes:

1. Patient demographic information
2. Primary diagnosis and ICD-10 code
3. Specialist information (name, NPI, tax ID)
4. Specific services requested
5. Medical necessity justification
6. Number of visits authorized
7. Timeframe for referral
8. PCP signature and date

Utilization Management

  • Pre-certification: Review before service is rendered
  • Concurrent Review: Review during hospital stay
  • Retrospective Review: Review after service is provided
  • Case Management: Coordination for complex cases
  • Disease Management: Programs for chronic conditions

MRI of lumbar spine without contrast

CPT Code: 72148 | Expected Date: 04/15/2024

Primary Diagnosis: Low back pain (M54.5)

Secondary Diagnosis: Sciatica (M54.3)

How to Obtain Preauthorization

  1. Verify insurance requirements for specific service
  2. Gather clinical documentation supporting medical necessity
  3. Submit request via online portal, phone, or fax
  4. Obtain authorization number and document in patient record
  5. Provide authorization number to facility performing service
  6. Note expiration date and any limitations

Contract Components

  • Fee Schedule: Agreed-upon rates for services

  • Payment Terms: When and how payment is made
  • Termination Clause: How contract can be ended
  • Hold Harmless Clause: Protects patients from balance billing

Balance Billing Restrictions

Participating providers agree to accept the insurance payment as payment in full (minus patient cost-sharing). They cannot bill the patient for the difference between their usual charge and the insurance allowed amount.

10 Eligibility Verification

Eligibility Verification Process

  1. Collect Information: Get insurance card and copy front/back
  2. Verify Online: Use insurance provider portal (preferred method)
  3. Phone Verification: Call customer service if online unavailable
  4. Document: Record verification details in patient record
  5. Inform Patient: Explain benefits, copays, and any authorizations needed

Information to Verify

Coverage Status

  • Active or inactive coverage
  • Effective and termination dates

Benefits

  • Deductible status (met/remaining)
  • Copay/coinsurance amounts
  • Out-of-pocket maximum status

Authorization Requirements

  • Preauthorization needs for procedures

12 Affordable Care Act Impact

Key ACA Provisions Affecting Patient Access

  • Pre-existing Conditions: Cannot be denied coverage or charged more
  • Essential Health Benefits: All plans must cover 10 categories of services
  • Preventive Care: Covered at 100% with no cost-sharing
  • Young Adult Coverage: Children can stay on parent's plan until age 26
  • Individual Mandate: Requirement to have coverage (penalty eliminated in 2019)
  • Marketplaces: Health Insurance Exchanges for comparing/purchasing plans
  • Medicaid Expansion: States can expand eligibility to more low-income adults
  • Subsidies: Premium tax credits for qualifying individuals/families

1 ICD History & Medical Necessity

Historical Use of ICD in United States
  • ICD-6 (1948): First ICD version adapted for US use
  • ICD-8 (1968): Implemented with modifications (ICD-8-CM)
  • ICD-9-CM (1979): Clinical Modification added for more detail
  • ICD-10-CM (2015): Implemented October 1, 2015 (delayed from 2014)
  • Future: ICD-11 adopted by WHO in 2019, not yet implemented in US
Diagnostic Coding & Medical Necessity

Diagnostic codes establish medical necessity - the justification that a service or procedure is appropriate and necessary for diagnosis or treatment of a medical condition.

Medical Necessity Components

  • Appropriate for symptoms/diagnosis
  • Provided for diagnosis or direct treatment
  • Consistent with standards of good medical practice
  • Not primarily for convenience of patient/provider
  • Not more costly than alternative services

2 ICD-10-CM Structure & Format

ICD-10-CM Code Structure

A 0 0 1 1

Characters 1-3: Category

Broad classification of disease/injury

Example: S72 - Fracture of femur

Character 4: Etiology

Cause, anatomic site, severity, or other detail

Example: S72.0 - Fracture of neck of femur

Characters 5-7: Extension

Episode of care, laterality, specifics

Example: S72.001A - Fracture of unspecified part of neck of right femur, initial encounter

ICD-10-CM Code Format Rules

  • Codes are alphanumeric (letters A-Z except U, numbers 0-9)
  • First character is always a letter
  • Second character is always a number
  • Codes may be 3-7 characters long
  • Decimal placed after third character
  • Codes must be used to highest specificity available

3 Using the Alphabetic Index

Alphabetic Index Organization

Steps for Using Alphabetic Index

  1. Identify Main Term: Usually the disease, condition, or anatomical site
  2. Locate Main Term: Find it in alphabetical order
  3. Review Subterms: Read indented terms under main term
  4. Follow Cross-references: See "see," "see also," "see category"
  5. Note Code Ranges: May see code ranges (E08-E13)
  6. Verify in Tabular List: NEVER code directly from Alphabetic Index

Alphabetic Index Example:

Diabetes
- mellitus
-- type 1E10.-
-- type 2E11.-
-- with ketoacidosisE10.10
--- type 1E10.10
--- type 2E11.10
-- with renal complicationsE10.21
see also hyperglycemia

Essential Modifiers

  • Subterms: Indented terms that modify the main term
  • Nonessential Modifiers: In parentheses, don't affect code selection but provide clarity
  • Carryover Lines: Continuation of previous line when term is too long
  • Notes: Important instructions for coding

4 Using the Tabular List & Coding Conventions

Tabular List Organization
  • Chapters: 21 chapters based on etiology or body system
  • Blocks: Groups of related categories within chapters
  • Categories: Three-character codes representing single conditions
  • Subcategories: Four to seven characters providing more specificity
  • Supplementary Sections: V, W, X, Y codes and Z codes
Coding Conventions
Convention Symbol Meaning Example
Includes None Terms that are part of the code description "Includes: acute bronchitis" under J20
Excludes1 Excludes1 NOT CODED HERE - mutually exclusive Type 1 diabetes excludes Type 2
Excludes2 Excludes2 NOT INCLUDED HERE - may be coded separately Diabetes with complications excludes without complications
Code also Code also Additional code required for complete picture Code underlying condition with manifestations
Use additional code Use additional code Add secondary code for greater specificity Add external cause code with injury
Code first Code first Underlying condition coded before manifestation Code diabetes first, then renal complication

Tabular List Navigation Rules

  1. Always verify Alphabetic Index codes in Tabular List
  2. Read all instructional notes (includes, excludes, code first, etc.)
  3. Code to highest specificity (use all available characters)
  4. Follow chapter-specific guidelines
  5. Never code a category without checking for fourth+ characters

5 Official Coding Guidelines

Key Official Coding Guidelines

General Coding Guidelines

  • Use both Index and Tabular List: Never code from Index alone
  • Code to highest specificity: Use all characters available
  • Signs and Symptoms: Code when no definitive diagnosis
  • Chronic Conditions: Code all that affect current care
  • "Rule Out" Diagnoses: Do not code - code signs/symptoms instead
  • Acute/Chronic Conditions: Code both if both exist

Selection of Principal Diagnosis

Principal Diagnosis Definition:

"The condition established after study to be chiefly responsible for occasioning the admission of the patient to the hospital for care."

Outpatient/Office Setting:

"The condition chiefly responsible for the outpatient service."

Multiple Coding Guidelines

Combination Codes

Single code that identifies two diagnoses or diagnosis with complication

Example: E11.21 - Type 2 diabetes with diabetic nephropathy

Etiology/Manifestation

Code first underlying disease, then manifestation

Example: E11.39 (Type 2 diabetes with ophthalmic complications) + H36.0 (Diabetic retinopathy)

6 Abstracting Diagnostic Statements

Steps for Abstracting Diagnostic Information

  1. Review Entire Record: Read all documentation related to encounter
  2. Identify All Conditions: List every diagnosis, symptom, finding
  3. Determine Primary Reason: What brought patient in today?
  4. Note Specificity: Type, site, laterality, severity, chronicity
  5. Check Documentation: Ensure diagnoses are documented by provider
  6. Query if Needed: Ask provider for clarification if documentation unclear
Abstracting Example

Provider Note: "Patient presents with acute low back pain radiating to right leg, onset 2 days ago after lifting heavy boxes. History of type 2 diabetes, hypertension. Suspected L4-L5 disc herniation. Prescribed NSAIDs and muscle relaxant. Follow up in 1 week."

Abstracted Diagnoses:

  1. Low back pain (acute)
  2. Sciatica (right side)
  3. Suspected disc herniation L4-L5
  4. Type 2 diabetes mellitus (chronic, affecting care)
  5. Hypertension (chronic, affecting care)

Coding Guidelines & Ethical Standards

9 Coding Guidelines Importance

Why Coding Guidelines Matter

For Accuracy

  • Consistency: Same codes for same conditions across healthcare
  • Specificity: Ensures detailed clinical picture
  • Comparability: Allows data comparison across facilities/time
  • Quality Measurement: Supports outcome tracking and research

For Reimbursement

  • Medical Necessity: Links services to appropriate diagnoses
  • DRG Assignment: Inpatient payment based on diagnosis codes
  • Risk Adjustment: Proper coding affects risk scores and payments
  • Claim Denials: Incorrect coding leads to claim rejections

Special Rules & Considerations

Laterality

Right, left, bilateral - must specify when available

Example: M17.11 (right), M17.12 (left)

Episode of Care

Initial encounter (A), subsequent (D), sequela (S)

Example: S52.501A (initial), S52.501D (subsequent)

Specificity Hierarchy

Unknown > unspecified > other specified

Example: Use "other specified" when more detail known

10 Medical Coding Ethical Standards

AHIMA Code of Ethics Principles

Accuracy & Integrity

  • Code only documented conditions
  • Never alter documentation to justify codes
  • Report coding errors when discovered
  • Maintain coding quality over quantity

Compliance

  • Follow all coding guidelines and conventions
  • Adhere to federal/state regulations
  • Respect patient privacy (HIPAA)
  • Report suspected fraud or abuse

Professionalism

  • Maintain and enhance professional skills
  • Avoid conflicts of interest
  • Respect confidential information
  • Provide accurate information to stakeholders

Common Ethical Dilemmas in Coding

  1. Upcoding: Assigning higher-level codes than documented
  2. Unbundling: Separating bundled services for higher reimbursement
  3. Cloning: Copying documentation without patient-specific details
  4. Query Pressure: Being pressured to code for higher reimbursement
  5. Diagnosis Overstatement: Exaggerating severity for payment

Best Practices for Ethical Coding

  • Code based on provider documentation only
  • Query providers for clarification when needed
  • Document all coding decisions and queries
  • Participate in regular compliance training
  • Use official coding guidelines as primary reference
  • Report concerns through proper channels
