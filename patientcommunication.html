Hardware Components

Understanding input, output, and storage devices.

Input vs. Output Devices

Device Type Definition Healthcare Examples
Input Devices Send data TO the computer
  • Keyboard & Mouse
  • Scanner (document, barcode)
  • Digital pen/stylus
  • Microphone (voice recognition)
  • Webcam (telemedicine)
Output Devices Receive data FROM the computer
  • Monitor/Display
  • Printer (labels, reports)
  • Speakers (alerts, patient education)
  • Projector (training)
Input/Output Devices Both input and output
  • Touchscreen monitor
  • Network interface card
  • Modem/router

Storage Devices & Cloud Storage

Primary Storage

RAM (Random Access Memory): Temporary, fast, volatile

ROM (Read Only Memory): Permanent, non-volatile

Volatile = loses data when power off

Secondary Storage

Hard Disk Drive (HDD): Magnetic storage, slower, cheaper

Solid State Drive (SSD): Flash memory, faster, more reliable

External Drives: USB flash drives, external HDD/SSD

Cloud Storage

Remote storage accessed via internet:

  • Advantages: Accessibility, backup, collaboration
  • Healthcare Use: Must be HIPAA-compliant
  • Examples: AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure
  • Security: Encryption, access controls required

Maintenance & Ergonomics

Proper care of computer equipment and workstation setup.

Computer Hardware Maintenance

Cleaning & Disinfection

General Rules:

  • Turn off and unplug before cleaning
  • Use approved disinfectants (check manufacturer)
  • Avoid excessive moisture
  • Clean keyboards and mice daily
  • Use screen-safe cleaners for monitors

Infection Control

Healthcare Specific:

  • Wash hands before/after computer use
  • Use keyboard covers that can be disinfected
  • Consider antimicrobial keyboards
  • Clean shared devices between users
  • Follow facility-specific protocols

Preventive Maintenance

Regular Tasks:

  • Regular software updates
  • Disk cleanup and defragmentation
  • Backup verification
  • Virus/malware scans
  • Check for wear and tear

Ergonomics Principles

Proper workstation setup to prevent injury and increase productivity:

Chair Position

Adjust height so feet are flat, thighs parallel to floor

Monitor Placement

Top of screen at or slightly below eye level, 20-40 inches away

Keyboard & Mouse

Elbows at 90-110 degrees, wrists straight

Lighting

Reduce glare, use task lighting as needed

Posture Breaks

Take micro-breaks every 30 minutes, stretch regularly

Document Placement

Use document holder at same height/distance as monitor

EMR vs. EHR Systems

Understanding electronic medical records and their differences.

Key Differences

Aspect Electronic Medical Record (EMR) Electronic Health Record (EHR)
Scope Digital version of paper chart in ONE practice Comprehensive record across MULTIPLE providers
Sharing Stays within the practice Designed to be shared with other providers
Focus Diagnosis and treatment in one practice Total health of patient across continuum
Interoperability Limited ability to exchange information Designed for information exchange
Patient Access May not include patient portal Often includes patient portal access

Hybrid Health Records

What is a Hybrid Record?

A combination of paper and electronic records during transition periods.

Common Scenarios:

  • Transitioning from paper to electronic
  • Scanning old records into new system
  • Temporary system during EHR implementation
  • Backup system during outages

Challenges & Solutions

Challenges:

  • Inconsistent data access
  • Duplicate documentation
  • Increased risk of errors
  • HIPAA compliance issues

Solutions: Clear protocols, staff training, timeline for full transition

Writing Fundamentals

Essential writing skills for professional communication.

Fundamental Writing Skills

Clarity

Use clear, concise language. Avoid medical jargon when communicating with patients.

Accuracy

Verify all information (names, dates, dosages) before sending.

Professional Tone

Maintain formal, respectful language appropriate for healthcare.

Organization

Logical flow with clear introduction, body, and conclusion.

Proofreading

Always review for spelling, grammar, and punctuation errors.

Business Writing Guidelines

Capitalization Rules

Capitalize: Proper nouns, titles before names, medical terms derived from proper nouns
Do NOT capitalize: General medical terms, job titles after names
Example: "Dr. Smith, our cardiologist, diagnosed atrial fibrillation."

Number Usage

Spell out: Numbers one through nine
Use numerals: Numbers 10 and above, ages, measurements, dosages
Medical exception: Always use numerals for dosages and lab values
Example: "Take 2 tablets every 4 hours for seven days."

Punctuation

Commas: Separate items in lists, clauses, and after introductory phrases
Semicolons: Connect related independent clauses
Colons: Introduce lists or explanations
Apostrophes: Show possession (doctor's office) or contractions

Business Letters

Professional correspondence in healthcare.

Components of a Business Letter

1. Letterhead - Practice name, address, contact information
2. Date - Date letter is written
3. Inside Address - Recipient's name and address
4. Salutation - Formal greeting (Dear Dr. Smith:)
5. Body - Main content, divided into paragraphs
6. Complimentary Close - Formal closing (Sincerely,)
7. Signature Block - Space for signature, typed name, title
8. Reference Initials - Writer/typist initials (JS/mt)
9. Enclosure Notation - If documents are included (Enclosure: Lab Report)
10. Copy Notation - If others receive copies (cc: Medical Records Department)

Letter Formats

Block Format

All elements aligned left, no indentation

Most common in healthcare

Advantages: Professional, easy to format

Modified Block Format

Date, closing, and signature centered or right-aligned

Advantages: Traditional, balanced appearance

Simplified Format

No salutation or closing, subject line emphasized

Less common in healthcare

Use: Internal memos, routine correspondence

Email, Fax & Mail

Professional communication methods in healthcare.

Memorandums & Professional Emails

Memorandums (Memos)

Internal communication within organization

  • To: Recipient(s)
  • From: Sender
  • Date: Current date
  • Subject: Brief description
  • Body: Concise message

Use: Policy updates, meeting announcements, internal notifications

Email Etiquette

  • Subject line: Clear and specific
  • Greeting: Formal salutation
  • Professional tone: Avoid slang, emoticons
  • Conciseness: Get to the point
  • Signature: Include name, title, contact
  • Proofread: Check before sending
  • Attachments: Mention in body
  • Response time: Within 24 hours for business

HIPAA-Compliant Fax Cover Sheets

Essential Elements of HIPAA Fax Cover Sheet

1. Confidentiality Notice: Warning about protected health information

2. Recipient Information: Name, fax number, contact person

3. Sender Information: Name, fax number, contact person

4. Date and Time: When fax was sent

5. Number of Pages: Including cover sheet

6. Purpose/Subject: Brief description of contents

7. Instructions if misdirected: "If received in error, destroy and notify sender"

Sample Confidentiality Statement:

CONFIDENTIAL: This fax contains protected health information (PHI) as defined by HIPAA. It is intended only for the designated recipient. If you are not the intended recipient, you are hereby notified that any disclosure, copying, distribution, or use of this information is strictly prohibited. Please notify the sender immediately and destroy all copies.

Mail Services & Processing

Envelope Addressing

  • Use recipient's formal title
  • Include complete address
  • Place return address in top left corner
  • Use proper postage
  • For confidential mail: Mark "CONFIDENTIAL"

Folding Documents

  • Standard #10 envelope: Fold in thirds
  • Small envelope: Fold in half then thirds
  • Fold with printed side inward
  • Avoid creasing through text
  • Use letter-folding tools for efficiency

Postal Services

  • First-Class Mail: Standard business correspondence
  • Certified Mail: Proof of mailing and delivery
  • Return Receipt: Proof recipient received
  • Priority Mail: Faster delivery (1-3 days)
  • Registered Mail: Most secure, for valuable items

Incoming Mail Processing

The medical assistant's role with incoming mail:

1. Sort and Distribute

Separate patient mail, bills, insurance correspondence, professional journals

2. Date Stamp

Stamp all incoming mail with date received

3. Open and Review

Open envelopes (except marked "Personal" or "Confidential")

4. Attach to Charts

Attach correspondence to appropriate patient charts

5. Prioritize Urgent Items

Flag time-sensitive items (lab results, consultation reports)

6. Document in Log

Maintain mail log for tracking purposes
