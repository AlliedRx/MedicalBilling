The Power of First Impressions

Understand how quickly impressions form and their impact on patient trust.

The 7-30 Second Rule

Research shows first impressions form within 7-30 seconds and are difficult to change. In healthcare, this affects:

Patient Trust

Patients decide early if they feel safe and respected

Communication Openness

Initial impression affects how much information patients share

Treatment Adherence

Positive impressions lead to better follow-through with care plans

Elements of Positive First Impressions

Professional Appearance

Clean, appropriate attire and grooming

Warm Greeting

Smile, eye contact, and using patient's name

Clear Introduction

Stating your name and role clearly

Confident Demeanor

Upright posture, calm voice, purposeful movements

Respecting Diversity

Understanding and demonstrating respect for individual differences in healthcare.

Dimensions of Diversity

Healthcare professionals must provide culturally competent care to all patients:

Gender

Using preferred pronouns and names; respecting gender identity

Race & Ethnicity

Acknowledging cultural health beliefs and practices

Religion

Accommodating religious practices and dietary restrictions

Age

Adapting communication for different life stages

Economic Status

Being sensitive to financial constraints in treatment plans

Appearance

Avoiding assumptions based on clothing, tattoos, piercings, etc.

Ability

ADA compliance; accommodating physical and cognitive differences

Language

Using interpreters; avoiding medical jargon

ADA Considerations

The Americans with Disabilities Act Amendments Act (ADAAA) requires:

  • Reasonable accommodations for patients with disabilities
  • Effective communication (sign language interpreters, Braille materials, etc.)
  • Accessible facilities and equipment
  • No discrimination based on disability

Example: For a patient who is deaf, appropriate accommodations might include a sign language interpreter, written communication, or video remote interpreting services.

Nonverbal Communication

Understanding the unspoken messages we send and receive.

The 7-38-55 Rule

Research suggests communication is interpreted as:

7%

Words

The actual verbal content

38%

Vocal Tone

How the words are spoken

55%

Body Language

Facial expressions, gestures, posture

Note: While the exact percentages are debated, the principle remains: nonverbal cues are extremely important in communication.

Types of Nonverbal Communication

Facial Expressions

Smiles, frowns, raised eyebrows

Gestures

Hand movements, pointing, nodding

Proxemics

Personal space and distance

Posture

Body positioning and orientation

Eye Contact

Cultural variations in appropriate eye contact

Haptics

Appropriate therapeutic touch

Chronemics

Use of time (punctuality, rushed vs. relaxed)

Paralanguage

Tone, pitch, volume, rate of speech

Communication Styles

Identifying different communication approaches and their impacts.

The Communication Cycle

Effective communication follows a continuous cycle:

1. Sender
Encodes message
2. Message
Verbal & nonverbal
3. Receiver
Decodes message
4. Feedback
Response completes cycle

Barriers can interrupt the cycle: noise, distractions, language differences, emotional states, cultural differences.

Communication Style Comparison

Assertive

Characteristics: Direct, honest, respectful expression of thoughts and feelings while considering others.

Example: "I understand you're busy, but I need clarification on this procedure before we continue."

Healthcare Impact: Most effective for clear communication and building trust.

Aggressive

Characteristics: Hostile, demanding, disrespectful of others' rights and feelings.

Example: "Just do what I say! I don't have time for your questions!"

Healthcare Impact: Creates fear, decreases patient cooperation, increases conflict.

Passive

Characteristics: Indirect, avoids conflict, doesn't express own needs or opinions.

Example: "It's fine, I'll figure it out myself..." (when confused about instructions)

Healthcare Impact: Leads to misunderstandings, errors, and unmet patient needs.

Passive-Aggressive

Characteristics: Indirect expression of hostility through sarcasm, backhanded compliments, or subtle sabotage.

Example: "Sure, I'll get to your request right away..." (then deliberately delays)

Healthcare Impact: Creates toxic environment, erodes team trust.

Manipulative

Characteristics: Uses guilt, flattery, or deception to control others and get needs met.

Example: "If you really cared about patients, you'd let me leave early today."

Healthcare Impact: Damages professional relationships, unethical.

Confidentiality

Therapeutic Techniques

Mastering the art of active listening and effective questioning.

Therapeutic Communication

Therapeutic communication is an interaction between a healthcare professional and a patient that enhances the patient's well-being. It builds trust and makes the patient feel valued.

Active Listening

Active listening involves giving full attention to the speaker, understanding the message, and responding thoughtfully.

Reflection

Repeating the main idea of the conversation while identifying the sender's feelings.

Restatement

Paraphrasing or repeating the patient's statements to confirm understanding.

Clarification

Asking questions to clear up confusion or get more details.

Question Types

Open-Ended Questions

Encourage detailed answers. Often start with "How", "What", or "Tell me about..."

"How have you been feeling since your last visit?"

Closed Questions

Can be answered with "Yes" or "No" or a single word. Used for specific facts.

"Does your throat hurt?"

Barriers & Theories

Overcoming obstacles and understanding human behavior theories.

  • Language Differences

  • Perception & Cultural Differences

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Patients must meet basic needs before addressing higher-level needs.

5. Self-Actualization (Personal Growth)
4. Esteem (Respect, Status)
3. Love/Belonging (Family, Connection)
2. Safety (Security, Health)
1. Physiological (Food, Water, Shelter)

Kbler-Ross Stages of Grief

Understanding these stages helps in communicating with terminally ill patients.

1
Denial
"No, not me."
2
Anger
"Why me?"
3
Bargaining
"Yes, but..."
4
Depression
"It's me!"
5
Acceptance
"It's part of life."

Professional Boundaries

Maintaining appropriate limits in the healthcare provider-patient relationship.

Self-Boundaries

Self-boundaries define the limits of appropriate professional behavior. They protect both the patient and the professional.

  • Keep the focus on the patient's needs, not your own.
  • Maintain physical and emotional distance appropriate for a professional relationship.
  • Avoid becoming "friends" with patients on social media.

Inappropriate Topics

To maintain professionalism, avoid discussing:

Politics

Religion

Personal Problems

Defense Mechanisms

Unconscious mental processes used to protect oneself from anxiety or pain.

Common Defense Mechanisms

Denial

Refusing to accept reality.

Displacement

Transferring feelings to a less threatening target.

Projection

Attributing one's own unacceptable feelings to others.

Repression

Blocking painful thoughts from the conscious mind.

Regression

Returning to an earlier stage of development.

Coping Mechanisms

Adaptive

Healthy ways of dealing with stress (e.g., exercise, talking to a friend, problem-solving).

Maladaptive

Unhealthy ways of dealing with stress (e.g., substance abuse, withdrawal, aggression).
