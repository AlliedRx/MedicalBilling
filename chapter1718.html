3 Special Reports & Modifiers

Special Reports

Required when services are unusual, complex, or require special documentation to justify medical necessity.

When Special Reports Are Needed

  • Unusual services or procedures
  • Variable or unusual time requirements
  • Unusual service coordination
  • Unusual equipment or supplies
  • Extraordinary circumstances
Importance of Modifiers

Modifiers are two-digit codes added to CPT codes to provide additional information about services without changing the basic definition.

-25

Significant, Separately Identifiable E/M Service

E/M service on same day as procedure

-50

Bilateral Procedure

Procedure performed on both sides

-59

Distinct Procedural Service

Separate procedure from others performed

-LT

Left Side

Procedure performed on left side

-RT

Right Side

Procedure performed on right side

-TC

Technical Component

Only technical portion of service

5 Documentation Requirements

Required Medical Documentation for Accurate Coding

  • Chief Complaint: Reason for visit in patient's own words
  • History of Present Illness: Chronology, severity, modifying factors
  • Review of Systems: Pertinent positive and negative findings
  • Past/Family/Social History: Relevant medical and social history
  • Physical Examination: Findings organized by body systems
  • Medical Decision Making: Diagnosis, data review, risk assessment
  • Procedure Details: Specific technique, approach, findings
  • Diagnosis/Impression: Final diagnosis and treatment plan
  • Provider Signature: Legible signature with credentials and date

Documentation Principles

  • If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done
  • Documentation must support medical necessity
  • Legible and complete entries
  • Timely documentation (same day service)
  • Use standardized formats (SOAP, etc.)
  • Avoid cloning or copying previous notes

6 Efficient CPT Coding Process

Steps for Efficient CPT Coding

  1. Review Documentation: Read entire encounter note
  2. Identify Services: List all procedures and services performed
  3. Use Alphabetic Index: Look up main terms for each service
  4. Verify in Tabular List: Read full code description and notes
  5. Apply Guidelines: Follow section-specific instructions
  6. Add Modifiers: Append appropriate modifiers if needed
  7. Check Bundling: Review CCI edits for unbundling issues
  8. Assign Codes: Finalize code selection
  9. Document Rationale: Note coding decisions in record

Using Alphabetic Index Effectively

Example: Coding a tonsillectomy

1. Main term: Tonsillectomy
2. Find entry: "Tonsillectomy"
3. Review subterms: May see "and adenoidectomy"
4. Code range: 42820-42836
5. Note: Check for age-specific codes

Using Tabular List Effectively

Example: Verifying code 42820

1. Find Surgery section > Respiratory System
2. Locate 42820 in numerical order
3. Read full description: "Tonsillectomy, primary or secondary; age 12 or over"
4. Check for notes: See also 42825 for under age 12
5. Verify parenthetical notes and cross-references

8 Anesthesia Coding Guidelines

Anesthesia Code Structure

Base Units

Relative value assigned to each anesthesia code (00100-01999)

Example: 00100 = 5 base units

Time Units

Calculated from anesthesia start to end (15-minute increments)

Formula: Total minutes √∑ 15 = time units

Modifying Units

Added for physical status (P1-P6) and qualifying circumstances

Example: P3 = +1 unit, Emergency = +2 units

Physical Status Modifiers

  • P1: Normal healthy patient
  • P2: Patient with mild systemic disease
  • P3: Patient with severe systemic disease
  • P4: Patient with severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to life
  • P5: Moribund patient not expected to survive without operation
  • P6: Declared brain-dead patient for organ donation

9 Surgical Coding Guidelines

Surgical Package Concept

Most surgical codes include preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative services.

Included in Surgical Package

  • Local infiltration, metacarpal/digital block, or topical anesthesia
  • Subsequent to decision for surgery, one related E/M encounter
  • Immediate postoperative care (dictating operative notes)
  • Writing orders
  • Evaluating patient in post-anesthesia recovery area
  • Typical postoperative follow-up care

Separately Reportable Services

  • Significant, separately identifiable E/M service (modifier 25)
  • Procedures by different physicians
  • Distinct procedural services (modifier 59)
  • Return to operating room for related procedure
  • Unrelated E/M service during postoperative period
  • Treatment of complications (does not require return to OR)

10 Specialized System Coding

Integumentary System

  • Lesion Removal: Size, number, method (excision, destruction)
  • Repair: Simple, intermediate, complex (length in cm)
  • Skin Grafts: Type, size, site preparation
  • Debridement: Depth, surface area

Musculoskeletal System

  • Fracture Care: Open vs closed treatment, with/without manipulation
  • Arthroscopy: Diagnostic vs surgical, specific procedures
  • Spinal Procedures: Approach (anterior/posterior), levels
  • Laterality: Always code right, left, or bilateral

Maternity Care & Delivery

  • Global Obstetric Package: Prenatal, delivery, postpartum care
  • Vaginal vs Cesarean: Different code families
  • Trimesters: Important for certain procedures
  • Multiple Gestations: Add-on codes for each additional fetus

11 Radiology, Pathology, & Medicine

Radiology Guidelines
  • Professional vs Technical Component: Modifiers -26 and -TC
  • Multiple Studies: Separate codes for each distinct study
  • Contrast Material: Separate codes for injection/administration
  • Supervision & Interpretation: Separate from procedure
Pathology & Laboratory Guidelines
  • Organ or Disease Panels: Grouped tests with single code
  • Standalone Tests: Individual test codes
  • Qualitative vs Quantitative: Different code families
  • Modifier -91: Repeat clinical diagnostic laboratory test
Medicine Section Guidelines
  • Vaccines: Product (90476-90749) vs administration (90460-90474)
  • Injections: Substance vs administration
  • Physical Medicine: Time-based codes (15-minute units)
  • Special Services: Case management, team conferences

2 Managed Care Policies & Patient Communication

Precertification/Preauthorization Process

  1. Identify services requiring authorization per insurance policy
  2. Gather clinical documentation supporting medical necessity
  3. Submit request via appropriate method (online, fax, phone)
  4. Obtain authorization number and document in patient record
  5. Communicate authorization details to patient and provider
  6. Note expiration date and visit limitations

Referral Process

  • HMO Plans: Require PCP referral for specialist visits
  • POS Plans: Referrals needed for in-network benefits
  • PPO Plans: Typically don't require referrals
  • Documentation: Keep copy of referral in patient chart
  • Time Limits: Referrals often have expiration dates

Sensitive Communication with Patients

  • Clear Explanation: Use simple, non-technical language
  • Empathy: Acknowledge frustration with insurance processes
  • Transparency: Explain costs and coverage limitations upfront
  • Written Materials: Provide brochures or handouts when available
  • Alternative Options: Discuss payment plans or financial assistance
  • Active Listening: Allow patients to express concerns

3 Steps for Filing Third-Party Claims

Claim Filing Process

  1. Encounter Documentation: Complete provider notes with all services
  2. Coding: Assign accurate ICD-10-CM, CPT, and HCPCS codes
  3. Charge Entry: Enter charges with correct codes and modifiers
  4. Claim Generation: System creates CMS-1500 or electronic equivalent
  5. Claim Scrubbing: Automated edit checking for errors
  6. Claim Submission: Send electronically or via paper
  7. Tracking: Monitor submission and follow up as needed
  8. Payment Posting: Apply payments and adjustments when received
  9. Denial Management: Address rejections and resubmit if appropriate

Timely Filing Requirements

  • Medicare: 1 year from date of service
  • Medicaid: Varies by state (typically 6-12 months)
  • Commercial: Varies by payer (typically 90 days - 1 year)
  • Important: Check each payer's specific requirements
  • Best Practice: Submit claims within 7-14 days of service

4 Submitting Health Insurance Claims

Electronic Claims Submission

  • Direct Submission: Via clearinghouse or payer portal
  • Clearinghouse: Intermediate processor that checks/edit claims
  • EDI Standards: HIPAA-compliant 837P format
  • Advantages: Faster processing, fewer errors, instant acknowledgments
  • Requirements: Payer enrollment, software compatibility

Paper Claims Submission

  • Form: CMS-1500 (02/12 version)
  • Method: Mail or fax
  • Use When: Electronic submission not available/possible
  • Disadvantages: Slower processing, higher error rates
  • Requirements: Correct form version, legible completion

Third-Party Payer Variations

  • Medicare: Electronic submission required (with exceptions)
  • Medicaid: State-specific requirements and portals
  • Commercial: Varies by insurance company
  • Workers' Comp: State-specific forms and requirements
  • Auto Insurance: Different forms and documentation needs

5 CMS-1500 Completion Guidelines

Key CMS-1500 Completion Guidelines

  • Form Version: Use current version (02/12)
  • Typeface: All capital letters, OCR-readable font
  • Format: Black ink on white paper, no corrections
  • Required Fields: Complete all applicable fields
  • Accuracy: Double-check all data before submission
üìã Critical CMS-1500 Fields
  1. Patient Information: Name, DOB, sex, address (fields 1a-13)
  2. Insurance Information: Subscriber details, policy numbers (fields 1, 1a, 4-9)
  3. Diagnosis Codes: ICD-10-CM codes in field 21
  4. Procedure Codes: CPT/HCPCS codes with modifiers in field 24D
  5. Dates of Service: From and to dates in field 24A
  6. Charges: Professional fees in field 24F
  7. Provider Information: NPI, tax ID, signature (fields 24J, 25, 31)
  8. Assignment: Accept assignment indicator in field 27

Common Completion Errors to Avoid

  • Incorrect patient/subscriber identification numbers
  • Missing or invalid diagnosis/procedure codes
  • Incorrect date formats
  • Missing required signatures
  • Illegible handwriting
  • Using wrong form version
  • Incomplete service line information

6 Fraud vs. Abuse

Fraud

Definition: Intentional deception or misrepresentation with knowledge that the deception could result in unauthorized benefit.

Examples:

  • Billing for services not rendered
  • Upcoding (billing for higher-level service)
  • Unbundling (billing separately for bundled services)
  • Falsifying diagnoses to justify services
  • Kickbacks for patient referrals

Consequences: Criminal penalties, fines, imprisonment, exclusion from programs

Abuse

Definition: Practices that are inconsistent with sound medical, business, or fiscal practices and result in unnecessary costs.

Examples:

  • Excessive charges for services
  • Providing medically unnecessary services
  • Improper coding (not intentional fraud)
  • Failure to maintain adequate records
  • Misusing codes on claim

Consequences: Civil penalties, repayment requirements, education

Preventing Fraud and Abuse

  • Implement comprehensive compliance program
  • Regular staff training on coding and billing
  • Internal and external audits
  • Clear documentation policies
  • Reporting mechanisms for suspected issues
  • Stay current with coding and billing regulations

7 Preventing Claim Rejection

Methods to Prevent Claim Rejection

  1. Front-End Verification: Check eligibility and benefits before service
  2. Accurate Documentation: Ensure complete and legible records
  3. Proper Coding: Use current codes with correct specificity
  4. Claim Scrubbing: Use software to catch errors before submission
  5. Timely Submission: Submit within payer deadlines
  6. Authorization Compliance: Obtain required preauthorizations
  7. Complete Forms: Fill all required fields on claim forms
  8. Provider Credentialing: Ensure providers are enrolled with payers

Common Rejection Reasons & Solutions

  • Invalid Member ID: Verify insurance card information
  • Non-covered Service: Check benefits before providing service
  • Missing/Invalid Diagnosis: Ensure diagnosis supports medical necessity
  • Duplicate Claim: Track submissions to avoid resending same claim
  • Timely Filing Issue: Monitor and submit claims promptly
  • Coordination of Benefits: Verify primary vs secondary insurance

8 Communicating with Providers

Tactful Communication with Medical Providers

  • Respect Expertise: Acknowledge provider's clinical knowledge
  • Focus on Education: Frame as learning opportunity, not criticism
  • Use Specific Examples: Reference actual cases rather than generalizations
  • Provide Solutions: Offer alternatives or suggestions for improvement
  • Document Conversations: Keep records of discussions and agreements
  • Choose Appropriate Timing: Don't interrupt patient care for billing issues
  • Be Collaborative: Work together to find mutually acceptable solutions

9 Checking Claim Status

Electronic Methods

  • Payer Portals: Online access to claim status
  • EDI 276/277: Electronic status inquiry and response
  • Clearinghouse Tools: Status tracking through intermediaries
  • Practice Management Systems: Integrated tracking features

Manual Methods

  • Phone Inquiries: Call payer customer service
  • Fax Requests: Submit status inquiry forms
  • Mail Correspondence: Written requests for status
  • On-site Visits: For complex or unresolved issues

Information Needed

  • Patient name and DOB
  • Provider name and NPI
  • Date(s) of service

Best Practices for Claim Follow-up

  • Establish standard follow-up schedule (e.g., 30, 60, 90 days)
  • Use automated tracking systems when possible
  • Document all status inquiries and responses
  • Escalate unresolved claims according to office policy
  • Identify patterns in delayed or denied claims

12 Medical Necessity Guidelines

Definition of Medical Necessity

"Health care services or supplies needed to prevent, diagnose, or treat an illness, injury, condition, disease, or its symptoms and that meet accepted standards of medicine."

Key Components of Medical Necessity

Appropriateness

Service is appropriate for symptoms/diagnosis

Consistent with diagnosis and standard of care

Reasonableness

Not excessive in scope, duration, or frequency

Least intensive/costly appropriate setting

Effectiveness

Expected to improve patient's condition

Not experimental or investigational

Following Medical Necessity Guidelines

  1. Document Thoroughly: Clear link between diagnosis and service
  2. Use Specific Codes: Most specific ICD-10-CM codes available
  3. Check Payer Policies: Review local coverage determinations (LCDs)
  4. Obtain Authorizations: When required by payer policy
  5. Use ABNs: Advance Beneficiary Notice for potentially non-covered services
  6. Appeal Appropriately: When medical necessity is challenged

13 Patient Financial Obligations

Patient Financial Responsibilities

  • Deductibles: Amount patient pays before insurance begins
  • Co-payments: Fixed amounts for specific services
  • Co-insurance: Percentage of costs after deductible
  • Non-covered Services: Services excluded from plan
  • Balance Billing: When provider doesn't accept assignment
  • Out-of-Network Costs: Higher costs for non-network providers

Informing Patients of Obligations

  • Verbal Explanation: Clear, simple language
  • Written Estimates: Good Faith Estimates under No Surprises Act
  • Insurance Verification: Share benefit details with patient
  • Payment Policies: Explain office payment requirements
  • Financial Assistance: Inform about available options

Sensitive Communication About Financial Matters

  • Privacy: Discuss in private setting
  • Empathy: Acknowledge financial concerns
  • Clarity: Avoid medical/insurance jargon
  • Options: Present payment plans or assistance
  • Timing: Discuss costs before non-emergency services
  • Documentation: Keep records of financial discussions
