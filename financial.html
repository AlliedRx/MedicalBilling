1 Bookkeeping Fundamentals

Bookkeeping Definition

The systematic recording, organizing, and maintaining of financial transactions for a business.

Accounts Receivable (A/R)

Money owed to the practice by patients and insurance companies

Accounts Payable (A/P)

Money the practice owes to vendors and suppliers

General Ledger

Complete record of all financial transactions

Chart of Accounts

List of all accounts used in the general ledger

Debit and Credit

Debit = left side (assets increase), Credit = right side (liabilities increase)

Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Each transaction affects at least two accounts (debit and credit)

Transactions in Patient Accounts
  • Charges: Services rendered and fees applied
  • Payments: Cash, checks, credit cards received
  • Adjustments: Write-offs, discounts, corrections
  • Refunds: Money returned to patients or insurers
  • Transfers: Moving balances between accounts
  • Bad Debt Write-offs: Uncollectible amounts removed

2 Accounts Receivable Procedures

Posting Transactions to Patient Accounts

  1. Charges: Enter services with CPT codes and fees
  2. Insurance Payments: Post EOB payments with adjustments
  3. Patient Payments: Apply co-pays, deductibles, balances
  4. Adjustments: Record write-offs, discounts, corrections
  5. Verify: Ensure accounts balance and match supporting documents

Payment at Time of Service

  • Co-payments: Collected at check-in per insurance requirements
  • Past Due Balances: Request payment on outstanding amounts
  • Self-Pay Patients: Collect estimated payment or full fee
  • High-Deductible Plans: Collect estimated patient portion

3 Truth in Lending Act Impact

Truth in Lending Act (Regulation Z)

Federal law requiring clear disclosure of credit terms to consumers.

Impact on Medical Practice Collections

Written Agreements Required

Payment plans exceeding 4 installments or charging interest must have written agreement

Disclosure Requirements

Must disclose finance charges, annual percentage rate (APR), payment schedule

Right of Rescission

Patients may have right to cancel agreement within 3 business days

Best Practices for TILA Compliance

  • Use standardized payment plan agreements
  • Clearly state all terms in writing
  • Provide copies to patients
  • Train staff on requirements
  • Consult legal counsel for complex arrangements

Adjustments to Patient Accounts

NSF Checks

  • Charge NSF fee per state law and office policy
  • Contact patient for alternative payment
  • Document communication attempts
  • Consider certified funds requirement for future

Collection Agency Transactions

  • Write off balance when sent to collections
  • Maintain record of agency and date sent
  • Do not continue billing patient directly
  • Document any payments received from agency

  5. File supporting documents (EOBs, receipts)
  6. Reconcile daily batches

6 Banking Fundamentals

Federal Reserve Bank System

The central banking system of the United States, established to provide stable monetary and financial system.

Federal Reserve Functions

  • Conducts national monetary policy
  • Supervises and regulates banks
  • Maintains financial system stability
  • Provides banking services to government

Types of Banks Managed

  • National Banks: Chartered by federal government
  • State Banks: Chartered by state governments
  • Member Banks: Belong to Federal Reserve System
  • Non-member Banks: State banks not in Fed system

Check Acceptance Precautions

  • Verify current date (not post-dated or stale-dated)
  • Confirm amount in numbers and words match
  • Check for proper signature
  • Verify patient identification
  • Consider check verification services for large amounts
  • Establish office policies for check acceptance limits

Check Processing Procedure

  1. Patient writes check to healthcare provider
  2. Provider deposits check into bank account
  3. Bank sends check through clearing system
  4. Federal Reserve processes between banks
  5. Funds transferred from patient's to provider's account
  6. Process typically takes 1-3 business days

NSF Check Procedure

  1. Contact patient immediately upon notification
  2. Request alternative payment method
  3. Charge NSF fee according to state law and office policy
  4. Document all communication attempts
  5. Consider requiring certified funds for future payments
  6. If unresolved, send to collections per office policy

8 Cash Acceptance Precautions

Cash Handling Security

Verification Procedures

  • Use counterfeit detection pen
  • Check security features (watermarks, security thread)
  • Verify large bills under UV light if available
  • Count cash twice - once when received, once when deposited

Security Measures

  • Keep minimal cash in drawer
  • Use drop safe for large amounts
  • Implement dual control for counting
  • Regular cash drawer audits
  • Surveillance cameras at cash handling areas

Documentation

  • Issue receipts for all cash transactions
  • Use numbered receipt books
  • Reconcile cash daily
  • Document discrepancies immediately
  • Maintain audit trail

9 Debit & Credit Cards

Debit Cards

Advantages:

  • Funds immediately available
  • Lower processing fees than credit cards
  • Reduced risk (requires PIN)
  • No credit check for patient

Precautions:

  • Verify signature matches card
  • Check expiration date
  • Use chip reader when possible
  • Keep receipts secure
Credit Cards

Advantages:

  • Guaranteed payment (if authorized)
  • Faster than check processing
  • Convenient for patients
  • Can process recurring payments

Precautions:

  • Higher processing fees (2-4%)
  • PCI compliance requirements
  • Chargeback risk
  • Secure storage of card information
Processing Security

PCI Compliance: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

  • Secure network and systems
  • Protect cardholder data
  • Regular vulnerability management
  • Strong access control measures
  • Regular monitoring and testing
  • Maintain information security policy

10 Ambulatory Care Banking Procedures

Daily Banking Procedures

Bank Deposit Preparation

  1. Count and verify all cash received
  2. Endorse all checks properly
  3. Complete deposit slip with totals
  4. Make copy of deposit for office records
  5. Place funds in secure deposit bag
  6. Make deposit before bank cutoff time
  7. Verify deposit receipt matches slip

Importance of Daily Deposits

  • Reduces theft risk from office
  • Accelerates funds availability
  • Improves cash flow management
  • Simplifies accounting reconciliation
  • Professional business practice
  • Reduces NSF check risk (deposit promptly)
Check Endorsements

Blank Endorsement

Simple signature on back
Risk: Anyone can cash if lost

Restrictive Endorsement

"For deposit only" + signature
Safety: Can only be deposited

Special Endorsement

"Pay to the order of [name]" + signature
Use: Transferring to another party

Check Writing Procedures
  1. Verify bill accuracy before payment
  2. Use secure check stock with security features
  3. Complete all fields clearly and accurately
  4. Obtain proper authorization signatures
  5. Record check in check register immediately
  6. Attach supporting documentation
  7. Mail promptly or pay electronically if possible

11 Bank Reconciliation & Bill Payment

Bank Account Reconciliation

Monthly process comparing bank statement with practice records to ensure accuracy.

Reconciliation Steps

  1. Compare ending balance on bank statement with practice records
  2. Check off cleared checks and deposits
  3. Add deposits in transit (not on statement yet)
  4. Subtract outstanding checks (not cleared yet)
  5. Account for bank fees or interest
  6. Investigate and correct any discrepancies
  7. Document reconciliation with signature and date

Auditing Importance

  • Detects errors in recording
  • Identifies fraudulent activity
  • Ensures accurate financial reporting
  • Required for tax preparation
  • Provides internal control
  • Supports compliance with regulations
Maximizing Cash Flow Through Bill Payment

Payment Timing Strategies

  • Take advantage of early payment discounts
  • Pay on due date (not early) to retain cash
  • Schedule payments to align with cash inflows
  • Use electronic payments for precise timing

Vendor Management

  • Negotiate favorable payment terms
  • Consolidate vendors when possible
  • Regularly review vendor performance and costs
  • Establish automated payment systems

Cash Flow Monitoring

  • Maintain cash flow projections
  • Monitor accounts receivable aging
  • Track key financial ratios
  • Regular financial review meetings

2 Conducting Effective Staff Meetings

Effective Meeting Structure

  1. Preparation: Create agenda, gather materials, notify participants
  2. Start on Time: Respect everyone's schedule
  3. Review Agenda: Outline topics and time allocations
  4. Facilitate Discussion: Encourage participation, manage time
  5. Document Decisions: Record action items and responsibilities
  6. Summarize: Review key points and next steps
  7. Follow Up: Distribute minutes, monitor action items
