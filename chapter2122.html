Lesson 21.1: Medical Emergencies

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify common healthcare emergencies and describe team roles during emergency response
  • Recognize and describe the use of emergency equipment and supplies
  • Administer first aid for environmental emergencies, burns, poisonings, anaphylaxis, bites/stings, and eye injuries
  • Recognize and respond to diabetic emergencies, including insulin shock
  • Identify musculoskeletal and neurological emergencies and provide first aid for seizures
  • Respond to respiratory emergencies and provide first aid for choking
  • Manage cardiovascular emergencies and provide appropriate first aid including CPR

Module 1: Introduction to Emergencies in Healthcare

A. Types of Emergencies in Healthcare Settings

  • Cardiovascular: Cardiac arrest, heart attack, stroke
  • Respiratory: Choking, asthma attack, respiratory arrest
  • Neurological: Seizures, syncope (fainting), altered mental status
  • Traumatic: Falls, fractures, bleeding wounds
  • Metabolic: Diabetic emergencies, electrolyte imbalances
  • Environmental: Burns, poisoning, temperature-related issues
  • Allergic: Anaphylaxis from medications, food, or environmental triggers

B. Emergency Response Roles

Each healthcare setting follows specific protocols (e.g., Code Blue, Rapid Response Team).

Role Responsibilities
First Responder Activates emergency response, begins initial assessment, starts basic life support
Team Leader Coordinates the response, assigns roles, makes key decisions, communicates with other teams
Airway Manager Manages airway, provides ventilation, prepares for intubation if needed
Compressor Performs high-quality chest compressions during CPR
Medication Nurse Administers emergency medications as directed, documents all medications given
Recorder/Timekeeper Documents all events, medications, interventions, and times; tracks compression cycles
Runner Retrieves equipment, medications, and supplies; communicates with lab/pharmacy

Note: In smaller facilities, team members may assume multiple roles. Always follow your facility's specific emergency response protocols.

Module 2: Emergency Equipment and Supplies

Essential Emergency Equipment

Automated External Defibrillator (AED)
Emergency Crash Cart
Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) resuscitator
Suction equipment (portable and wall-mounted)
Oxygen delivery systems (tanks, masks, nasal cannulas)
Splints and immobilization devices
Burn care supplies (sterile dressings, burn gels)
Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Crash Cart Contents

Emergency crash carts are standardized and typically contain:

  • Cardiac monitor/defibrillator
  • Emergency medications (epinephrine, atropine, amiodarone, etc.)
  • IV access supplies and fluids
  • Airway management equipment (endotracheal tubes, laryngoscope)
  • Emergency procedure trays (central line, chest tube)
  • Documentation forms

Important: Crash carts must be checked regularly (usually daily) for expired medications and complete supplies. A seal or log indicates when it was last checked.

Module 3: First Aid for Environmental Emergencies

Temperature-Related Emergencies

Heat Stroke: Hot, dry skin; confusion; rapid pulse; possible loss of consciousness

First Aid Steps:

  1. Move to cool area immediately
  2. Remove excess clothing
  3. Cool with water, ice packs, or wet sheets
  4. Fan the person while wetting skin
  5. Monitor breathing and consciousness
  6. Seek immediate medical attention

Hypothermia: Shivering, confusion, slurred speech, weak pulse, loss of coordination

First Aid Steps:

  1. Move to warm area
  2. Remove wet clothing
  3. Warm center of body first (chest, neck, head, groin)
  4. Use warm blankets, skin-to-skin contact
  5. Give warm (not hot) beverages if person is alert
  6. Handle gently - rough handling can cause cardiac arrest

Burns

Classifications:

  • First Degree: Red, painful, dry (sunburn)
  • Second Degree: Blisters, swelling, wet appearance
  • Third Degree: White or charred, leathery, may be painless (nerve damage)

First Aid for Burns:

  1. Stop the burning process (remove from source)
  2. Cool the burn with cool (not cold) running water for 10-20 minutes
  3. Remove jewelry or tight items before swelling occurs
  4. Cover with sterile, non-stick dressing
  5. Do not apply ice, butter, or ointments to severe burns
  6. Seek medical attention for severe burns, burns on face/hands/genitals, or chemical/electrical burns

Poisonings

General Signs: Nausea, vomiting, difficulty breathing, confusion, seizures, unconsciousness

First Aid Steps:

  1. Check the scene for safety (chemical fumes, etc.)
  2. Check responsiveness and breathing
  3. Call Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) or emergency services
  4. Have container/package available to identify substance
  5. Do NOT induce vomiting unless directed by medical professional
  6. If chemical is on skin, remove contaminated clothing and rinse skin for 15-20 minutes
  7. If poison is in eyes, rinse eyes with lukewarm water for 15-20 minutes

Module 4: Diabetic Emergencies

Insulin Shock (Hypoglycemia)

Causes: Too much insulin, too little food, excessive exercise

Signs & Symptoms: Confusion, irritability, sweating, trembling, rapid heartbeat, hunger, dizziness, seizures, loss of consciousness

First Aid for Conscious Patient:

  1. Give 15-20 grams of fast-acting sugar:
    • 4 oz fruit juice or regular soda
    • Glucose tablets/gel
    • 1 tablespoon sugar or honey
  2. Wait 15 minutes and recheck blood sugar if possible
  3. If still low, repeat sugar treatment
  4. Once improved, give complex carbohydrate and protein snack

First Aid for Unconscious Patient:

  1. Place in recovery position
  2. Do NOT give anything by mouth
  3. Call emergency services immediately
  4. Administer glucagon injection if trained and available
  5. Monitor breathing and pulse
  6. Be prepared to perform CPR if needed

Module 5: Musculoskeletal and Neurologic Emergencies

Seizures

Types: Generalized (tonic-clonic), partial (focal), absence

First Aid During Seizure:

  1. Stay calm and time the seizure
  2. Protect from injury - move furniture, place pillow under head
  3. Turn person on their side (recovery position) to keep airway clear
  4. Loosen tight clothing around neck
  5. Do NOT restrain the person
  6. Do NOT put anything in their mouth
  7. Stay with them until fully alert

When to Call Emergency Services:

  1. Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
  2. Person has difficulty breathing afterwards
  3. Person is injured during seizure
  4. Person is pregnant or has diabetes
  5. Another seizure follows immediately
  6. Seizure occurs in water
  7. Person does not regain consciousness after seizure stops

Module 6: Respiratory Emergencies

Choking (Conscious Adult/Child)

Signs of Severe Choking: Cannot breathe, speak, or cough; universal choking sign (hands to throat); blue lips/nails

Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich Maneuver):

  1. Stand behind person, wrap arms around waist
  2. Make a fist with one hand, thumb side against middle of abdomen, above navel
  3. Grasp fist with other hand
  4. Give quick, inward and upward thrusts
  5. Continue until object is expelled or person becomes unconscious

For Pregnant or Obese Patients: Use chest thrusts instead of abdominal thrusts. Place hands at center of chest (same position as for CPR compressions).

For Unconscious Choking Victim:

  1. Lower to ground, call for help/activate emergency response
  2. Begin CPR starting with chest compressions
  3. Before giving breaths, check mouth for object and remove if visible
  4. Continue cycles of 30 compressions and 2 breaths

Module 7: Cardiovascular Emergencies

Bleeding Wounds

First Aid for Severe Bleeding:

  1. Apply direct pressure with sterile dressing or clean cloth
  2. Elevate injured area above heart if no fracture suspected
  3. Apply pressure to pressure points if direct pressure fails
  4. Apply tourniquet only as last resort for life-threatening limb bleeding
  5. Never remove soaked dressing - add more layers on top
  6. Treat for shock - keep person warm and lying down

Shock

Signs & Symptoms: Pale/cool/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, nausea, confusion, unconsciousness

First Aid for Shock:

  1. Call for emergency medical help
  2. Have person lie down on back
  3. Elevate legs about 12 inches (unless head/neck/leg injury suspected)
  4. Keep person warm with blanket
  5. Do not give anything to eat or drink
  6. Monitor breathing and consciousness
  7. Be prepared to perform CPR if needed

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)

Adult CPR Steps (C-A-B):

  1. Check for responsiveness and breathing
  2. Call for help/activate emergency response
  3. Compressions: Place heel of hand on center of chest, other hand on top
    • Push hard and fast (at least 2 inches deep)
    • Rate of 100-120 compressions per minute
    • Allow chest to recoil completely between compressions
  4. Airway: Tilt head back, lift chin to open airway
  5. Breathing: Give 2 breaths (1 second each), watching for chest rise
  6. Continue cycles of 30 compressions to 2 breaths
  7. Use AED as soon as available

Note: For infant/child CPR, compression depth is about ⅓ of chest depth. For infants, use two fingers or two-thumb encircling technique. Rescue breathing for children/infants is particularly important.

Important Disclaimer: This lesson provides general first aid information. Always follow your facility's specific protocols and your scope of practice. Proper certification (BLS, ACLS, First Aid) is required to perform many of these skills in clinical settings.
