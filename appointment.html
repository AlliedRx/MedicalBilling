1 Appointment Schedule Guidelines & Matrix Creation

Establishing Appointment Schedules

  • Analyze provider preferences and practice patterns
  • Consider patient population needs and appointment types
  • Determine optimal appointment lengths for different visit types
  • Build in buffer time for emergencies and overruns
  • Schedule complex procedures during high-energy times
  • Allow time for administrative tasks and breaks

3 Appointment Book Scheduling & Self-Scheduling

Method Description Considerations
Appointment Book Traditional paper-based scheduling system Limited to one user, prone to errors, no backup
Computerized System Digital scheduling software Multiple users, backups, integration with EHR
Self-Scheduling Patients schedule appointments online via portal Reduces phone calls, 24/7 access for patients
Hybrid System Combination of methods based on patient preference Flexible but requires managing multiple systems
Self-Scheduling Benefits: Reduces front desk calls by 30-50%, increases patient satisfaction, provides 24/7 scheduling access, decreases no-show rates with automated reminders.

4 Legality of Appointment Scheduling Systems

Legal Considerations

  • HIPAA Compliance: Appointment systems must protect patient privacy
  • ADA Compliance: Schedule must accommodate patients with disabilities
  • Documentation: All scheduling changes must be documented
  • Abandonment: Cannot refuse to schedule medically necessary care
  • Emergency Care: Must have protocols for urgent/emergency scheduling
  • Retention: Appointment records must be kept per state regulations (typically 5-7 years)
Critical Note: Appointment systems cannot discriminate based on race, color, national origin, sex, age, or disability. All patients must have equal access to scheduling.

6 Telephone Scheduling & New Patient Information

Critical Information for New Patient Scheduling

Required Information Checklist:

  1. Full legal name (first, middle, last)
  2. Date of birth
  3. Contact information (phone, email, address)
  4. Insurance information (carrier, ID number, group number)
  5. Reason for visit (chief complaint)
  6. Referral source (if applicable)
  7. Emergency contact information
  8. Primary care provider information
  9. Previous medical records transfer authorization

Telephone Scheduling Protocol

"Thank you for calling Green Valley Medical Clinic. My name is Sarah. I'd be happy to schedule your appointment. First, I'll need to gather some basic information to get you registered in our system. May I have your full name and date of birth?"

9 Special Circumstances in Scheduling

Dealing with Consistently Late Patients

  • Policy Enforcement: Clearly communicate late policy at registration
  • Advanced Scheduling: Book late patients at end of day
  • Reminder Calls: Call day before emphasizing punctuality
  • Charges: Implement late fees (check state regulations)
  • Termination: As last resort, dismiss from practice with proper notice

Office Policies for Rescheduling

Rescheduling Protocol:

  1. Require 24-hour notice for non-emergency cancellations
  2. Document reason for rescheduling in patient record
  3. Offer alternative appointment times immediately
  4. Flag frequent reschedulers for follow-up
  5. Implement cancellation fees for repeated no-notice cancellations

Emergencies, Referrals & Walk-Ins

  • Emergencies: Always accommodated immediately or directed to ER
  • Provider Referrals: Given priority scheduling within appropriate timeframe
  • Patients without Appointments: Triage for urgency; schedule for next available if non-urgent

10 Failed Appointments & No-Shows

Handling Failed Appointments

  • Document all no-shows in patient record
  • Call patient immediately to reschedule
  • Send follow-up letter for repeated no-shows
  • Consider dismissal after 3 consecutive no-shows
  • Report no-shows to referring providers when appropriate

Increasing Appointment Show Rates

Reminder Systems

Automated calls, texts, emails 24-48 hours before appointment

Confirmation Calls

Personal call 1-2 days prior to confirm

New Patient Packets

Send complete information before first visit

Flexible Scheduling

Offer early morning, evening, weekend appointments

Transportation Assistance

Provide information about transportation options

Incentive Programs

Small rewards for on-time attendance

11 Handling Cancellations & Delays

⏰ Cancellation Management Protocol
  1. Document cancellation reason and date/time of call
  2. Offer alternative appointment immediately
  3. Fill vacant slot from waitlist if available
  4. Update provider schedule and notify clinical staff
  5. If late cancellation (<24 hours), apply policy consistently
  6. Send confirmation for newly scheduled appointment

Managing Provider Delays

  • Inform patients as soon as delay is known
  • Provide realistic wait time estimates
  • Offer rescheduling if wait exceeds 30 minutes
  • Keep patients updated every 15 minutes
  • Provide comfortable waiting area with amenities
  • Apologize sincerely for the inconvenience

12 Patient Arrival Preparation & Check-In

Daily Preparation Checklist:

  • Review schedule for day's appointments
  • Pull/prepare charts for scheduled patients
  • Confirm provider availability and special needs
  • Prepare new patient packets if needed
  • Ensure waiting area is clean and organized
  • Test check-in equipment (computers, scanners, card readers)
  • Have necessary forms ready (consents, updates, etc.)

Patient Check-In Procedure

  1. Greet patient by name with smile
  2. Verify identity with two identifiers
  3. Update demographic and insurance information
  4. Collect co-pays and outstanding balances
  5. Provide required forms to complete/update
  6. Notify clinical staff of patient arrival
  7. Provide wait time estimate
  8. Escort to exam room when ready

13 Patient Reception & Processing

Showing Consideration for Patients' Time

  • Acknowledge wait time upon arrival
  • Provide accurate wait time estimates
  • Apologize for delays sincerely
  • Keep patients informed of schedule changes
  • Offer to reschedule if wait is excessive
  • Thank patients for their patience

Patients with Special Needs

Mobility Issues

Provide wheelchair assistance, ground floor exam rooms

Hearing Impaired

Face patient when speaking, written instructions, interpreter if needed

Visually Impaired

Verbal guidance, braille materials if available, describe surroundings

Cognitive Disabilities

Simple instructions, repeat as needed, involve caregiver

Escorting & Instructing Patients

"Mrs. Johnson, Dr. Smith is ready for you now. Please follow me to exam room 3. You can have a seat on the exam table, and the medical assistant will be in shortly to take your vital signs. Do you need any assistance getting up on the table?"

Health Record Placement

  • Paper charts: Outside exam room door in designated holder
  • EHR: Computer on cart outside room or inside for documentation
  • Never leave records unattended in public areas
  • Secure records when not in use (locked cabinets for paper, logged out for EHR)

1 Two Types of Patient Records

Paper Medical Records

  • Physical documents in folders/files
  • Handwritten or typed entries
  • Stored in filing cabinets
  • Require physical space for storage
  • Single user access at a time
  • Vulnerable to damage/loss

Electronic Health Records (EHR)

  • Digital records in computer systems
  • Multiple access points simultaneously
  • Secure cloud or server storage
  • Automated backups and recovery
  • Integration with other systems
  • Advanced search capabilities

2 Importance of Accurate Health Records

Clinical Care

4 Ownership of Health Records

Legal Ownership

  • Physical Record: Owned by healthcare provider/facility that created it
  • Information Content: Patient has right to access and control disclosure
  • HIPAA Rights: Patients have right to inspect, copy, and amend their records
  • Transfer: Records transferred when care is transferred to new provider
  • Retention: Provider must maintain records for period required by state law
Key Point: While the provider owns the physical or electronic record, the patient owns the information within it and has legal rights to access and control its distribution.

5 EHR vs. EMR Distinction

Electronic Medical Record (EMR)
  • Digital version of paper chart in one practice
  • Primarily used by providers for diagnosis and treatment
  • Does not easily travel outside the practice
  • Focuses on clinical data from that practice only
Electronic Health Record (EHR)
  • Comprehensive patient health information
  • Designed to be shared across healthcare organizations
  • Includes data from all clinicians involved in care
  • Patient can access their own EHR through portals
  • Supports broader view of patient's health journey

6 Meaningful Use & Healthcare Legislation

Meaningful Use Definition

Meaningful Use is a CMS program that provides financial incentives for healthcare providers who demonstrate "meaningful use" of certified EHR technology to improve patient care.

Three Main Components of Meaningful Use:

  1. Use of Certified EHR Technology in a meaningful manner
  2. Electronic Exchange of Health Information to improve quality of care
  3. Submission of Clinical Quality Measures to CMS

Meaningful Use Objectives

  • Improve quality, safety, efficiency
  • Engage patients and families
  • Improve care coordination
  • Improve population and public health
  • Ensure privacy and security protections

7 Nonverbal Communication with EHR Systems

The "Screen Facing" Challenge

When using EHR systems during patient encounters, providers may unintentionally focus on the computer screen rather than maintaining eye contact with patients, which can negatively impact the patient-provider relationship.

Strategies for Maintaining Connection

  • Positioning: Place computer so you can see both screen and patient
  • Verbal Cues: Explain what you're doing: "I'm just entering your medication list"
  • Eye Contact: Look up from screen frequently
  • Body Language: Face patient when possible, avoid turning back
  • Involve Patient: Show them information on screen when appropriate
  • Timing: Do documentation during natural breaks in conversation

8 EHR Backup Systems & Record Management

EHR Backup Systems

  • Daily Incremental Backups: Changes since last backup
  • Weekly Full Backups: Complete system backup
  • Off-site Storage: Physical or cloud-based backup
  • Disaster Recovery Plan: Procedures for system failure
  • Testing: Regular restoration testing of backups

Transfer, Destruction & Retention

Transfer

Secure electronic transfer with audit trail. Paper records copied and mailed certified.

Destruction

Secure electronic deletion with verification. Paper records shredded with certificate of destruction.

Retention

Adult records: 7-10 years from last visit. Minor records: Until age of majority + statute of limitations.

9 Paper Record Retention & Destruction

Paper Record Retention Requirements

  • Varies by state law (typically 5-10 years after last visit)
  • Minor records: Keep until patient reaches age of majority + statute of limitations
  • Medicare/Medicaid: Minimum 5 years from date of service
  • Workers' Compensation: Varies by state, often 5-30 years
  • When in doubt, retain longer rather than shorter

Secure Destruction Methods

  • Shredding: Cross-cut or micro-cut shredding for confidentiality
  • Pulping: Paper converted to pulp for recycling
  • Burning: Incineration in controlled facilities
  • Documentation: Maintain certificate of destruction
  • Witness: Have staff witness destruction process

10 Releasing Health Information & HIEs

When to Release Health Information

  • With patient's written authorization (signed release form)
  • For treatment, payment, or healthcare operations (TPO)
  • As required by law (court order, subpoena)
  • For public health activities (disease reporting)
  • To avert serious threat to health or safety
  • For workers' compensation claims

Health Information Exchanges (HIEs)

HIEs allow healthcare providers to securely share patient information electronically across different healthcare organizations.

Benefits

  • Reduces duplicate testing
  • Improves care coordination
  • Provides complete patient history
  • Enhances emergency care

Types

  • Directed Exchange: Secure messaging between providers
  • Query-Based Exchange: Providers search for patient information
  • Consumer-Mediated Exchange: Patients control their health information

12 Documentation & Corrections

Documentation Guidelines

EHR Documentation:

  • Use standardized templates when appropriate
  • Enter information promptly after encounter
  • Use accurate date/time stamps
  • Include author identification
  • Use objective, factual language

Making Corrections to Health Records

NEVER erase, white-out, or obliterate original entry

Paper Records: Draw single line through error, initial and date, write correction nearby

EHR Systems: Use amendment function, addendum with explanation, maintain audit trail

13 Dictation & Transcription

Dictation

  • Verbal recording of medical information
  • Digital voice recorders or phone-based systems
  • Used for complex notes, procedures, consultations
  • Must include patient identifiers and date

Transcription

  • Conversion of dictated audio to text
  • Medical transcriptionists or speech recognition software
  • Requires proofreading by dictator
  • Must maintain confidentiality during process
Modern Alternative: Speech recognition software integrated with EHR allows providers to dictate directly into patient records, with immediate text conversion and editing capabilities.

15 Indexing Rules & Record Creation

Indexing Rules (ARMA Guidelines)

  1. Index by surname first, then given name, then middle initial
  2. Consider nothing before something (Smith comes before Smithsonian)
  3. Disregard punctuation and special characters
  4. Treat hyphenated names as one unit
  5. Treat prefixes (Mc, Mac, Van) as part of the name
  6. Numbers before letters in alphanumeric systems

Creating a Patient Health Record

  1. Collect complete demographic information
  2. Create face sheet with patient identifiers
  3. Include consent and authorization forms
  4. Add problem list and medication list
  5. Organize sections according to chosen format
  6. Label record with unique identifier
  7. Enter into master patient index

16 Filing Methods Pros & Cons

Alphabetic Filing

Pros: Simple, direct access, no cross-reference needed

Cons: Misfiles common, expansion difficult

Numeric Filing

Pros: Confidentiality, unlimited expansion

Cons: Requires cross-reference, additional step

Terminal Digit Filing

Pros: Even distribution, efficient for large volumes

Cons: Training required, not intuitive

Color-Coded Filing

Pros: Visual accuracy check, reduces misfiles

Cons: Initial setup cost, color blindness considerations

17 File Organization & Correspondence

Organizing Files

  • Active Files: Current patients, easily accessible location
  • Inactive Files: Patients not seen in 3-5 years, storage area
  • Closed Files: Deceased or transferred patients, separate storage
  • Purged Files: Destroyed per retention schedule

Health-Related Correspondence Organization

  • File correspondence chronologically within patient record
  • Use separate sections for different correspondence types
  • Flag urgent or action-required correspondence
  • Maintain log of incoming/outgoing correspondence
  • Use tickler file for follow-up dates
