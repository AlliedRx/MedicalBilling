2 Inventory Procedures & Documentation

Inventory Process Steps

  1. Preparation: Gather inventory sheets, pens, clipboard, calculator
  2. Categorization: Separate items by type (medical supplies, office supplies, medications)
  3. Counting: Physically count each item in stock
  4. Recording: Document quantities on inventory sheet
  5. Verification: Double-count high-value or critical items
  6. Analysis: Compare counts to minimum/maximum levels
  7. Documentation: Complete inventory report with date and signature
  8. Action: Generate purchase orders for low stock items

3 Routine Equipment Maintenance

Purpose of Routine Maintenance

  • Preventive Care: Avoid equipment failures during patient care
  • Accuracy: Ensure diagnostic equipment provides correct readings
  • Longevity: Extend equipment lifespan and performance
  • Safety: Prevent accidents, electrical hazards, or contamination
  • Compliance: Meet manufacturer and regulatory requirements
  • Cost Savings: Reduce expensive repairs and replacements

Maintenance Schedule Examples

4 Warranties, Service & Purchasing

Warranties

  • Manufacturer's Warranty: Typically 1-3 years for defects
  • Extended Warranty: Optional additional coverage
  • Service Contracts: Pre-paid maintenance agreements
  • Documentation: Keep warranty cards and receipts
  • Exclusions: Understand what's not covered (abuse, neglect)

Service Calls

  • Document issue thoroughly before calling
  • Have model/serial numbers ready
  • Note error messages or symptoms
  • Ask about priority and estimated time
  • Get service report with findings and recommendations

Purchasing Equipment

  • Research options and compare features
  • Consider total cost of ownership
  • Check compatibility with existing systems
  • Review warranty and service options
  • Get demonstrations and references
  • Consider future needs and scalability

5 Inventory Management Systems

Inventory Control Systems

System Type Description Best For
Manual System Paper-based, visual checks, manual reordering Small practices, low-cost items
Par Level System Pre-set minimum/maximum quantities for each item Medium practices, predictable usage
Just-in-Time (JIT) Ordering as needed, minimal stock on hand Large practices with reliable suppliers
Computerized System Automated tracking, barcode scanning, automatic reordering Large practices, high-value items

Ordering Supplies Process

  1. Review inventory levels against par levels
  2. Create purchase order with item numbers and quantities
  3. Obtain necessary approvals per office policy
  4. Submit order to approved vendor
  5. Receive shipment and verify against packing slip
  6. Check for damage and expiration dates
  7. Restock shelves with new items behind old (FIFO)
  8. Update inventory records
  9. Process invoice for payment

  • Use step stools for high shelves, never climb on shelves
  • Request help for heavy or bulky items

7 Identifying Unsafe Working Conditions

Common Workplace Hazards in Healthcare

Physical Hazards

  • Wet floors without warning signs
  • Obstructed walkways and exits
  • Poor lighting in storage areas
  • Loose electrical cords
  • Unsecured heavy equipment

Biological Hazards

  • Improperly disposed sharps
  • Contaminated surfaces not cleaned
  • Unlabeled biohazard waste
  • Inadequate PPE availability
  • Improper hand hygiene stations

Ergonomic Hazards

  • Workstations at wrong height
  • Chairs without proper support
  • Repetitive motions without breaks
  • Lifting heavy patients without assistance
  • Improper computer monitor positioning

Reporting Procedure

  1. Document the hazard with location and description
  2. Take immediate action if possible (place warning sign, clean spill)
  3. Report to supervisor or safety officer
  4. Follow up to ensure correction
  5. Document the resolution

8 Emergency Response Plans

Critical Elements of Emergency Plans
  • Evacuation Routes: Clearly marked exits and alternative routes
  • Assembly Points: Designated safe areas away from building
  • Communication Plan: How to notify staff, patients, emergency services
  • Emergency Contacts: Updated list of key personnel and services
  • Patient Prioritization: System for evacuating non-ambulatory patients
  • Utility Shut-off: Locations and procedures for gas, water, electricity
  • Emergency Supplies: First aid kits, flashlights, batteries, water
  • Staff Roles: Assigned responsibilities during emergencies
  • Recovery Plan: Procedures for resuming operations

Mock Exposure Event Participation

Participating in drills helps identify gaps in emergency plans. During mock events:

  • Treat the drill as a real emergency
  • Follow assigned roles and procedures
  • Note timing and communication issues
  • Participate in the debriefing session
  • Provide constructive feedback for improvement

Fire Safety Priorities

  1. Rescue: Help anyone in immediate danger
  2. Alarm: Activate fire alarm and call 911
  3. Contain: Close doors to contain fire
  4. Extinguish/Evacuate: Use extinguisher if safe, otherwise evacuate

Remember: Only attempt to fight a fire if it's small, contained, and you have a clear escape route.

10 Effects of Emergency Situations

Physical Effects

  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure
  • Rapid breathing or hyperventilation
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Nausea or dizziness
  • Fatigue after adrenaline subsides
  • Headaches or muscle tension

Emotional/Psychological Effects

  • Anxiety, fear, or panic
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Irritability or anger
  • Withdrawal or emotional numbness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Sleep disturbances or nightmares

Support Strategies

  • Provide clear, calm instructions
  • Maintain a reassuring presence
  • Encourage slow, deep breathing
  • Offer water if appropriate
  • Provide follow-up support and debriefing
  • Connect with professional help if needed

Crisis Intervention for Medical Assistants

During emergencies, your calm demeanor can help stabilize others. Use simple, clear language. Acknowledge feelings without judgment. Focus on immediate needs and next steps rather than the overall situation.

1 Sources and Uses of Drugs

Drug Sources

Natural Sources

  • Plants: Digitalis (foxglove), morphine (poppy), quinine (cinchona bark)
  • Animals: Insulin (pancreas), thyroid hormones (thyroid glands)
  • Minerals: Iron supplements, magnesium, lithium
  • Microorganisms: Penicillin (mold), streptomycin (bacteria)

Synthetic & Semi-synthetic

  • Synthetic: Created entirely in laboratories (most modern drugs)
  • Semi-synthetic: Natural compounds chemically modified
  • Biotechnology: Genetically engineered (monoclonal antibodies)
Drug Uses
  • Therapeutic: Treat, cure, or prevent disease
  • Diagnostic: Aid in diagnosis (contrast media)
  • Palliative: Relieve symptoms without curing
  • Prophylactic: Prevent disease (vaccines, antibiotics before surgery)
  • Replacement: Replace deficient substances (hormones, enzymes)

2 Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Process Definition Factors Influencing Clinical Significance
Absorption Drug movement from administration site into bloodstream Route, solubility, blood flow, pH, food interactions Determines onset of action and bioavailability
Distribution Drug movement from blood to tissues Blood flow, protein binding, lipid solubility, barriers (BBB) Affects drug concentration at site of action
Metabolism Chemical alteration of drug in the body (mainly liver) Liver function, age, genetics, other drugs, disease states Creates active/inactive metabolites, affects duration
Excretion Removal of drug from body (mainly kidneys) Kidney function, urine pH, age, other drugs Determines elimination rate and dosing interval

3 Drug Action & Effects

Factors Influencing Drug Action
  • Age: Metabolism differs in children and elderly
  • Weight/Body Composition: Affects drug distribution
  • Gender: Differences in body composition and hormones
  • Genetic Factors: Enzyme variations affecting metabolism
  • Disease States: Liver/kidney disease affects metabolism/excretion
  • Psychological Factors: Placebo effect, patient expectations
  • Environment: Temperature, altitude, noise
  • Route of Administration: Affects absorption rate and bioavailability
  • Time of Administration: Circadian rhythms affect drug action
Therapeutic vs. Adverse Effects

Therapeutic Effects

Intended, beneficial effects of medication

  • Analgesia (pain relief)
  • Antibacterial action
  • Blood pressure reduction
  • Blood glucose control

Adverse Reactions

Unintended, harmful effects

  • Side Effects: Predictable, dose-related (nausea, drowsiness)
  • Allergic Reactions: Immune response (rash, anaphylaxis)
  • Toxic Effects: Excessive dosing (liver/kidney damage)
  • Idiosyncratic Reactions: Unusual, unpredictable responses

4 Drug Legislation & Dietary Supplements

Key Drug Legislation

  • Pure Food and Drug Act (1906): Prohibited misbranded/adulterated drugs
  • Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (1938): Required proof of safety before marketing
  • Controlled Substances Act (1970): Established drug schedules (I-V)
  • Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (1994): Regulated supplements as foods, not drugs
  • Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996): Protected patient health information
  • Drug Addiction Treatment Act (2000): Allowed office-based opioid treatment

Dietary Supplements

  • Regulated as foods, not drugs (FDA oversight differs)
  • Cannot claim to treat, cure, or prevent disease
  • Manufacturers responsible for safety, not FDA pre-approval
  • Can interact with prescription medications
  • Medical assistants should document all supplements in patient records
  • Quality varies between manufacturers

7 Medication Classifications

Antibiotics

Indications: Bacterial infections

Examples: Penicillin, Amoxicillin, Ciprofloxacin

Side Effects: Nausea, diarrhea, allergic reactions

Antihypertensives

Indications: High blood pressure

Examples: Lisinopril, Amlodipine, Hydrochlorothiazide

Side Effects: Dizziness, cough, electrolyte imbalance

Analgesics

Indications: Pain relief

Examples: Ibuprofen, Acetaminophen, Oxycodone

Side Effects: GI upset, liver toxicity, dependence

Antidiabetics

Indications: Diabetes mellitus

Examples: Metformin, Insulin, Glipizide

Side Effects: Hypoglycemia, GI distress, weight gain

Anticoagulants

Indications: Blood clot prevention

Examples: Warfarin, Heparin, Apixaban

Side Effects: Bleeding, bruising, interactions with food

Antidepressants

Indications: Depression, anxiety

Examples: Sertraline, Fluoxetine, Venlafaxine

Side Effects: Sexual dysfunction, weight changes, insomnia

8 Drug Terminology

Term Definition Clinical Significance Example
Onset of Action Time until drug begins to produce effect Determines when patient should feel relief Fast-acting insulin: 15-30 minutes
Peak Effect Time when drug reaches maximum therapeutic effect When monitoring for maximum benefit or side effects Regular insulin: 2-4 hours
Duration of Action Length of time drug produces therapeutic effect Determines dosing frequency Lisinopril: 24 hours (once daily dosing)
Biologic Half-life Time for drug concentration to decrease by 50% in body Determines steady state and accumulation risk Digoxin: 36-48 hours (long half-life)

Half-life Clinical Application

It takes approximately 5 half-lives for a drug to reach steady state (consistent blood levels) and 5 half-lives for a drug to be eliminated from the body after discontinuation. This is crucial for determining loading doses and when to monitor for therapeutic effects.

10 Prescription Parts & Refill Procedures

Four Parts of a Prescription

  1. Superscription: Patient information (name, DOB, address), date, Rx symbol (‚Ñû)
  2. Inscription: Drug name, strength, dosage form (e.g., "Lisinopril 10mg tablets")
  3. Subscription: Dispensing instructions to pharmacist (quantity, refills, instructions)
  4. Signature: Directions for patient (Sig: "Take 1 tablet daily"), provider signature, DEA# if controlled

Prescription Refill Procedures

  1. Verify patient identity and medication details
  2. Check remaining refills and expiration date
  3. Review patient chart for recent visits/lab results if needed
  4. Obtain provider approval if no refills remain or monitoring needed
  5. Document refill in patient record with date and initials
  6. Contact pharmacy with authorization
  7. Notify patient that refill has been approved

Commonly Approved Abbreviations

  • PO: By mouth
  • PR: Rectally
  • IM: Intramuscular
  • IV: Intravenous
  • Q: Every
  • BID: Twice daily
  • TID: Three times daily
  • QID: Four times daily
  • PRN: As needed
  • AC: Before meals
  • PC: After meals
  • HS: At bedtime

DEA Requirements for Controlled Substances

  • Maintain separate, secure storage (double-locked cabinet)
  • Document all transactions in controlled substance log
  • Conduct regular inventory counts
  • Report losses/theft immediately to DEA and local police
  • Maintain records for 2 years
  • Follow state-specific requirements which may be stricter

12 OTC Medications & Herbal Supplements

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Medications

  • Available without prescription for self-treatment
  • Generally recognized as safe and effective when used as directed
  • Examples: Acetaminophen (Tylenol), Ibuprofen (Advil), Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)
  • Can interact with prescription medications
  • Patients may not report OTC use unless specifically asked
  • Medical assistants should document all OTC use in patient records

Herbal Supplements

  • Considered dietary supplements, not medications
  • Not regulated for safety/efficacy like prescription drugs
  • Common examples: Echinacea, St. John's Wort, Ginkgo Biloba, Ginseng
  • Can have significant drug interactions (St. John's Wort with antidepressants)
  • Quality and potency vary between brands
  • Patients may believe "natural" means "safe"

Patient Education Points

  • Always inform healthcare providers about ALL medications and supplements
  • Read OTC labels carefully for active ingredients and warnings
  • Follow dosage instructions precisely
  • Be aware of potential interactions with prescription medications
  • Choose supplements from reputable manufacturers
  • Report any adverse reactions to healthcare provider
